# Sound Effects Story - Psychological Framework

**Child's Age / Description:** Liska, young snow leopard (approximately 5 years old)
**The Setting:** Evening in the yard, blankets spread around the well near a fire, with neighboring children and a few adults gathered for storytelling
**The Situation:** Liska is invited to provide sound effects for the neighbor storyteller's tale
**Target Emotion:** Performance anxiety - fear of making mistakes in front of others

---

### **Framework 1: Psychological Analysis (The "Why")**

**Core Emotion:** Performance anxiety - the worry about being judged and making mistakes when all eyes are watching

**Primary Drivers:**
1. **Evaluation Sensitivity:** At this age, children become acutely aware of being watched and judged, triggering freeze responses when the perceived stakes feel too high
2. **Perfectionism in Public:** The desire to do well transforms into fear of failure when performing for others, especially when the task feels important
3. **Physical Overwhelm:** Performance anxiety manifests strongly in the body - racing heart, tight throat, frozen muscles - making it hard to think or act

**Secondary Drivers:**
- Fear of disappointing the storyteller who chose them
- Comparison with other children who might do better
- Uncertainty about what constitutes a "good" sound effect

---

### **Framework 2: The Child's Subjective Experience (The "What")**

**Immediate Response (First Seconds - Pride):**
- "Me? They want ME!"
- "I'm the special helper!"
- "Stand up tall-tall!"
- "Everyone sees me!"
- "I know good sounds!"

**Primary Feelings (What Overwhelms Them - Anxiety Building):**
- "What if I do it wrong?"
- "Everyone watching me!"
- "Throat feels stuck"
- "Can't remember any sounds!"
- "Want to hide now!"

**Physical Sensations (How Their Body Feels - At Peak Anxiety):**
- "Heart going too fast"
- "Throat all tight-tight"
- "Legs feel shaky"
- "Tummy hurts"
- "Can't move right!"

**Secondary Reactions (After Freeze Response):**
- "Everyone still looking!"
- "Storyteller waiting for me"
- "Other kids know I'm stuck"
- "Want mommy"
- "Can't make ANY sound!"

**What They Notice (Environmental Awareness During Anxiety):**
- "Too many eyes watching"
- "Fire too bright"
- "Sounds too loud"
- "No place to hide"
- "Everyone too close"

**Summary:** The progression moves from initial pride at being chosen, through mounting anxiety as performance approaches, to complete freeze response when unable to make the expected sound, setting up the need for a coping strategy.

---

### **Framework 3: Narrative Implementation (The "How")**

**Environmental Description Setup (Showing Anxiety):**
- *The circle of watching faces seemed to grow bigger and closer, like a wall of eyes*
- *The fire's crackling sounded too loud, each pop making Liska's ears flatten*
- *Every blanket rustle and whisper felt magnified.
- *Even the familiar yard felt strange and exposed under all those watching eyes*

**Character's Physical Experience (Anxiety Manifestation):**
- *Liska's throat closed up tight, like someone tied a knot inside*
- *Her legs turned to wobbly sticks that didn't want to hold her up*
- *She opened her mouth but no sound came out, not even a tiny squeak*
- *Her heart hammered so hard she was sure everyone could hear it*
- *Her paws felt cold and tingly, like they'd forgotten how to work*


**Character's Thoughts/Words (During Anxiety Peak):**
- *"Can't make the sound! Can't make ANY sound!"*
- *"Everyone sees I can't do it!"*
- *"My voice is stuck-stuck-stuck!"*
- *"Want to run away but legs won't move!"*
- *"They'll think I'm a baby!"*

**The Coping Skill Introduction (Through Parent/Adult):**
- *"Look at my eyes, little one. Just my eyes, nobody else"*
- *"Feel your paws on the ground? Press them down, one-two"*
- *"Big breath to your belly, like blowing up a balloon"*
- *"Now just make any sound, even tiny. It's okay to start small"*
- *"Remember: eyes, feet, breath, then try"*

**Showing Success Through Learned Skill:**
- *Liska found Mama's warm eyes in the crowd and held on tight*
- *She pressed her paws down hard, feeling the earth solid underneath*
- *Her belly expanded with air, pushing out the tight-scared feeling*
- *A tiny "meep" escaped - not the right sound, but A sound!*
- *The second try came easier: "Mrow?" Then finally, a proper "RAWR!"*

**Sensory Vocabulary (Prioritized for this emotion):**
- Primary words: tight, stuck, frozen, shaky, wobbly, heavy, cold
- Supporting words: warm (for comfort), solid (for grounding), soft (for support), steady, calm

**Key Narrative Techniques:**
1. **Physical-First Anxiety:** Show the body's response before naming the emotion
2. **The Freeze Response:** Depict the paralysis authentically - can't speak, can't move
3. **Concrete Coping Steps:** Eyes-Feet-Breath-Try - a memorable sequence kids can use
4. **Graduated Success:** Start with any sound, build to the desired performance
5. **Progression Pattern:** Pride → Growing anxiety → Complete freeze → Learning skill → Small success → Full performance

### **The Coping Skill: "Eyes, Feet, Breath, Try"**

**What It Is:**
A four-step grounding technique specifically designed for performance anxiety in young children. Each step helps break the freeze response and reconnect the child with their body and ability to act.

**The Steps:**
1. **EYES:** Find one safe person's eyes to focus on (reduces overwhelming stimuli)
2. **FEET:** Press feet firmly into the ground (physical grounding)
3. **BREATH:** Take a big belly breath (activates parasympathetic nervous system)
4. **TRY:** Make any small attempt (breaks perfectionism, allows graduated success)

**Why It Works:**
- Simple enough to remember under stress
- Addresses both physical and emotional aspects of anxiety
- Provides concrete actions when feeling frozen
- Allows for imperfect first attempts
- Can be practiced and used in many situations

**Story Implementation:**
- Introduced by Mama when Liska freezes completely
- Practiced once with support
- Used independently for final successful performance
- Celebrated as Liska's new "performance helper" skill

**1. The Specific Emotions and Problems (Beyond the "Separation Anxiety" Label):**

For a 5-year-old, the anxiety isn't a single feeling. It's a cluster of concrete problems and fears that surface at specific times:

*   **Fear of the Unknown:** The camp counselors are new faces. The rules are different. The schedule (when to eat, when to sleep) is not her own. This loss of predictability is unsettling.
*   **Social Insecurity:** "Will the other kids like me?" "What if I'm not good at the games?" "What if everyone else already has a friend?" She may withdraw or become shy, even if she's normally outgoing.
*   **The Ache of Homesickness:** This is a physical feeling of longing. It's triggered by sensory details: the food tastes different, her cot feels strange, the woods smell unfamiliar. It's a deep-seated desire for the comfort of the known.
*   **Vulnerability at Key Moments:** The anxiety will peak during unstructured times or moments of transition. The two most potent are:
    *   **Mealtimes:** Sitting at a big, noisy table without her parents can feel chaotic and lonely.
    *   **Bedtime:** This is the ultimate crisis point. The day's distractions are gone. It's dark, the sounds are strange, and she is acutely aware that she is alone in a new place.

**2. The Narrative Arc: A Story of Self-Regulation**

The story isn't just about her being sad and then getting over it. It's a three-act structure of her first major act of self-soothing and problem-solving.

*   **Act 1: The Setup and The Gift**
    *   Liska is excited but nervous about camp. As her mother helps her pack, the first real pangs of worry appear.

She misses her mother's specific goodnight kiss, the feel of her own blanket. She starts to cry silently. This is the lowest point. She feels helpless.

*   **Act 3: The Turning Point and Resolution**
    *   In her sadness, her hand finds the tuft of fur on her belt. This is the turning point.
    *   She doesn't magically stop being sad. Instead, she *uses the tool*. She holds the fur and actively recalls her mother's words. She thinks: "A fox is an explorer. A fox is brave."

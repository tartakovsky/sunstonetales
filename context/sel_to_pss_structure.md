### Analysis: SEL Structure Compatibility with Problem-Solving Stories

The current five-act structure is **approximately 85% compatible** with problem-solving stories (often called "cognitive development stories" or "thinking skills narratives" in educational literature). The emotional regulation focus needs reframing toward cognitive challenge, but the core architecture remains sound.

**What Works Perfectly:**
- **Act 1 (Introduction)** - Identical usage. Sensory opening and character establishment work for any story type
- **Act 2.1-2.2 (Lead-up/Catalyst)** - Directly applicable. The "catalyst" becomes the problem presentation
- **Act 3's Trial-and-Error Structure** - The attempt/failure/recovery cycle maps perfectly onto problem-solving iterations
- **Act 4's Climax Pattern** - The "turning point" and "success" beats align with "aha moment" and "solution works"
- **Act 5's Integration** - Showing mastery through teaching others or applying skills elsewhere fits cognitive stories

**What Needs Reframing (Not Reworking):**

**Beat 2.3-2.4: Core Incident/Fallout**
- SEL: Emotional problem manifests → dysregulation
- Problem-Solving: Cognitive challenge revealed → confusion/frustration (mild)
- **Adjustment**: Keep structure, but "fallout" becomes "initial bafflement" - one line of "Huh?" not emotional spiral

**Beat 3.4: Low Point**
- SEL: Emotional nadir with potential breakdown
- Problem-Solving: "Stuck moment" - child realizes current approach won't work
- **Adjustment**: Replace emotional intensity with cognitive frustration ("This is too hard!"), keeping it brief

**Beat 3.5: Recovery**
- SEL: Deep co-regulation cycle
- Problem-Solving: Brief reset - might be self-directed ("Let me think...") or minimal adult prompt ("What else could work?")
- **Adjustment**: Shorten to 1-2 lines max, focus on cognitive reset not emotional soothing

**Beat 3.6: Re-evaluation**
- SEL: Emotional insight through scaffolding
- Problem-Solving: Cognitive breakthrough through observation/questioning
- **Adjustment**: Already perfect - just emphasize "noticing" over "feeling"

**Key Structural Difference:**
- SEL stories follow: Problem → Emotion → Regulation → Growth
- Problem-solving follows: Problem → Thinking → Discovery → Mastery
- The beats remain identical; only the content focus shifts

**Recommendation:**
Don't rework the structure. Instead, create a simple "content substitution guide":
- Replace "dysregulation" with "cognitive challenge"
- Replace "coping strategies" with "thinking strategies"
- Replace "co-regulation" with "cognitive scaffolding"
- Replace "emotional metaphors" with "concrete comparisons" (e.g., "like fitting puzzle pieces")

The modular design means you can use the exact same beat structure, just populate it with problem-solving content. This maintains architectural consistency while serving different developmental goals.

### The Resolution: Two Lenses, One Blueprint

Think of your 20-beat structure as an architectural blueprint for a house. This blueprint is solid - it has a foundation, load-bearing walls, a roof. It guarantees a sturdy building.

Right now, you're using this blueprint to build a **"Therapy & Wellness Center" (your SEL stories)**. Every room and feature is designed to support emotional health.
- The "Low Point" room is for catharsis.
- The "Recovery" room is for co-regulation and healing.

You want to use the same blueprint to build a **"Science & Engineering Lab" (your problem-solving stories)**. You don't need to tear down the walls. You just need to repurpose the rooms. The "Low Point" room isn't for crying; it's for hitting a complex equation. The "Recovery" room isn't for hugs; it's for stepping back to look at the whiteboard.

The structure stays the same. Its *function* changes.

### How to Implement This Without Over-Engineering: Three Simple Rules

1.  **Emotion is a By-product, Not the Driver.** In your SEL stories, emotion is the problem. In these new stories, the puzzle is the problem. Mild frustration might be a *symptom* of being stuck, but it's not the focus. A single line like "Liska’s ears drooped. This was harder than she thought" is enough.

2.  **Attempts Are for Data, Not Drama.** An "attempt" is not a story of failure; it's a story of information gathering. The first attempt isn't wrong, it just reveals that the problem is more complex. Reframe it this way:
    - Attempt 1: "Does pushing it work? No. Okay, now I know it's stuck."
    - Attempt 2: "Does using a stick work? No. Okay, now I know I need a different angle."

3.  **Collapse Beats Ruthlessly.** If a beat feels forced, shrink it to a single sentence or action. The "Thinking Space" (Beat 3.5) might just be: "She sat back on her heels and tilted her head, looking at the box from a new spot." That's it. You've hit the beat without slowing the story.

Your structure is a good *storytelling* blueprint. This guide allows you to use it for a different *teaching* purpose. It's not over-engineered if you treat it as a flexible checklist, not a rigid formula.

## Major Structural Issues:

1. **Coping Strategy Violation:** The tuft is given as magic solution without any practice or scaffolding. Violates the rule about introducing coping strategies.

<!-- That's realistic. If the child doesn't act out, which they sometimes don't when they're sad, nobody sees and co-regulates. -->
2. **Missing Co-Regulation:** Child manages massive separation anxiety essentially alone. Where are the camp counselors? Other supportive adults?

<!-- We scaffolded it with a story and a tuft. What else can we do? -->
3. **Emotional Complexity Beyond Age:** "Sad and excited at the same time" requires emotional differentiation skills beyond 5-year-old capacity without significant scaffolding.

<!-- Framework also accounts for co-regulation not always being realistic -->
4. **Recovery Without Support:** All recovery happens through self-regulation when framework emphasizes co-regulation for this age.

<!-- It's after -->
5. **Abstract Thinking in Dysregulation:** Moon-as-connection is beautiful but requires symbolic thinking unavailable during emotional flooding.

6. **No Practice Types Used:** Story jumps from problem to solution without any practice or preparation beats.

## Necessary Additions:

1. **Pre-camp preparation scene:** Where coping strategies are introduced and practiced
2. **Camp counselor co-regulation:** Adult support during the acute homesickness
3. **Concrete coping strategies:** Breathing, counting, movement - not just abstract concepts
4. **Peer support scaffolding:** Other children modeling coping, not just commiserating
5. **Simpler metaphors:** Age-appropriate ways to understand missing someone
6. **Gradual emotional transitions:** Not jumping from despair to excitement
7. **Authentic child dialogue:** How 5-year-olds actually talk about feelings

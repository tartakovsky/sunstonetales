## Act 1: Introduction
### Beat 1.1: Introduce setting and atmosphere
Text:
> The Sarabád market buzzed with colorful stalls and the smell of grilled fish and sweet melons. Liska, a little snow leopard, tried to look everywhere at once. Her tail swished with excitement in the salty breeze.

Review:
> **Critique:** The description is functional but could more strongly establish the market as a sensory labyrinth, which would pay off later when Liska gets lost. The current description is pleasant, but lacks a sense of overwhelming scale from a child's perspective.
>
> **Suggestion:** Intensify the sensory details to foreshadow the later feeling of being overwhelmed. Instead of a general "buzz," describe it as a "river of sounds and smells," mentioning a cacophony of overlapping noises (vendors calling out, bells ringing, strange animals bleating) to set up the sensory overload she'll experience when lost.

### Beat 1.2: Introduce character(s)
Text:
> Liska's tail swished with excitement. She darted a few steps toward a stall selling shiny blue glass, then remembered herself and scampered back to her mother, Mara. Mara laughed. "It's a big place, my little snowflake," she said, giving Liska's paw a squeeze. "What's our market rule?" Liska puffed up her chest. "Find a helper!" "That's right," her father, Daric, added. "Someone we know, or anyone working at a stall."

Review:
> **Critique:** The establishment of the "find a helper" rule is pedagogically excellent. However, Daric's phrasing, "Someone we know, or anyone working at a stall," introduces an "or" conjunction that creates a slightly complex cognitive load for a 4-6 year old, especially one who would be trying to recall the rule under stress.
>
> **Suggestion:** Simplify and concretize the rule. Mara asks, "What's our market rule?" Liska replies, "Find a helper!" Daric then reinforces this with a primed example: "That's right. Like the Kebab-Man, or anyone who is busy working at a stall." Using a known, concrete example (`the Kebab-Man`) first makes the abstract category (`anyone working at a stall`) easier to grasp and recall. This also primes her to think of him specifically later on.

## Act 2: Inciting Incident – The Challenge Begins
### Beat 2.1: Lead-up
Text:
> Liska's parents stopped at a pottery stall, lost in a serious talk about the subtle differences between eggshell and ecru-colored glazes. Liska waited, but soon her tail stopped swishing. The world of grown-ups was very slow.

Review:
> **Critique:** The transition from patient waiting to active boredom is slightly abrupt. The narrative states she becomes bored rather than showing the process.
>
> **Suggestion:** Show the process of boredom setting in through action. Liska could first attempt to engage with the boring adult world—perhaps by sniffing a pot, patting the stall owner's leg, or trying to balance on one foot—before her attention is captured elsewhere. This makes her subsequent impulsive departure a more natural progression from failed engagement to a new sensory target.

### Beat 2.2: The Catalyst (Misstep / Misjudgment / Accidental Event)
Text:
> Suddenly, a delicious smell danced past her nose. It was sizzling fish kebabs from the Kebab-Man's stall—her favorite! Her mouth watered, and without thinking, she followed the wonderful smell through a forest of tall legs.

Review:
> Excellent. This is a perfect depiction of impulse control in a 4-6-year-old. The sensory lure is strong and the action is not malicious, but a "misjudgment" based on an immediate desire. The "forest of adult legs" is a superb visual metaphor for her child-level perspective.

### Beat 2.3: The Core Incident (The External Problem / Challenge)
Text:
> She reached the kebab stall and turned to share her discovery, but saw only a sea of unfamiliar legs. Her parents were gone. Liska was lost.

Review:
> **Critique:** The blueprint's comment correctly identifies the ideal emotional sequence (Surprise -> Anxiety -> Fear), but this is meta-text. The narrative text itself is too direct and doesn't fully exploit the dramatic potential of this sequence.
>
> **Suggestion:** Rewrite the beat's `Text` to explicitly enact the emotional progression. "She reached the kebab stall, a happy grin on her face, and turned to share her discovery. But... her parents weren't there. Her grin faded. Her head tilted. She spun in a circle, her eyes darting between the unfamiliar legs. A cold, wobbly feeling started in her tummy. They were gone. Liska was lost." This "show, don't tell" approach makes the moment of realization far more impactful.

### Beat 2.4: Fallout
Text:
> A wave of fear made her feel tiny and helpless, like a little mouse in a big, scary world. Her heart thumped against her ribs. Her only thought was to hide.

Review:
> **Critique:** The `Little Mouse (Мышонок)` metaphor is strong and well-placed. The connection to the flight/hide response is clear.
>
> **Suggestion:** To amplify the somatic accuracy of the metaphor, add another sensory detail that connects the abstract concept to a physical sensation. "Her ears flattened against her head, and the thumping in her chest felt like a tiny, trapped bird trying to get out."

## Act 3: Attempts to Solve / Unravel – Facing the Problem & Inner Turmoil
### Beat 3.1: Recovery (Optional)
Text:
> This beat is folded into the initial attempt. Liska's instinctual "recovery" from the initial shock of fear is to perform a coping action: hiding.

Review:
> This is a sophisticated and accurate observation. The initial attempt *is* the coping mechanism, albeit a maladaptive one for the goal of being found. It correctly models that a child's first response is to seek immediate emotional safety, not to problem-solve logically.

### Beat 3.2: Attempt 1 (Initial Reaction / Less Considered Approach)
Text:
> Following her "Little Mouse" instinct, Liska scurried under the counter of the kebab stall. She curled up, hidden from the forest of legs. Being unseen made the world less scary, but it didn't solve the real problem.

Review:
> **Critique:** The final sentence is an adult's analytical conclusion delivered by the narrator. It would be stronger to show this realization through Liska's internal experience.
>
> **Suggestion:** Rephrase from Liska's perspective to distinguish between the resolution of the external sensory threat and the persistence of the internal emotional state. "She curled up, hidden from the forest of legs. The scary legs were gone. *Phew.* But the big, wobbly feeling of being alone hadn't gone anywhere. It was still there, trapped inside her."

### Beat 3.3: Attempt 2 (Developing Approach / New Complication)
Text:
> The initial relief of being hidden quickly fades. Under the counter, the world is dark and dusty. The happy sounds of the market are muffled and distant, making her feel even more alone. She can hear her own heart thumping nervously. Hiding wasn't safe, it was just... lonely. The big, scary feeling of being lost was still there, now trapped in the small, dark space with her.

Review:
> **Critique:** The conclusion "Hiding wasn't safe, it was just... lonely" is still slightly too analytical. The transition from "hiding feels better" to "hiding feels worse" requires a more concrete trigger to be fully believable.
>
> **Suggestion:** Use a specific sensory contrast to trigger the emotional shift. "Under the counter, it smelled like dust and old spices, not like Mama's warm fur. The happy sounds of the market were muffled and strange, making her feel like she was a thousand miles away." The direct sensory contrast with the safety of her parents (smell, sound) is what makes her realize this hiding spot is not truly safe.

### Beat 3.4: Low Point (Emotional & Practical Setback)
Text:
> A hopeless feeling washed over her, making her body feel limp like a deflating balloon. Curling into a tight ball, she pulled her tail over her eyes and whimpered. Mama and Papa were gone forever.

Review:
> **Critique:** The `Deflating Balloon (Сдувающийся Шарик)` metaphor is excellent for helplessness. The catastrophic thought ("gone forever") is age-appropriate. The two can be more tightly integrated.
>
> **Suggestion:** Connect the catastrophic thought directly to the physical sensation of the metaphor. "A hopeless feeling washed over her, and her body went limp, as if all the air had whooshed out of her. *Fshhhh...* went the little balloon of hope inside her. She curled into a tight ball, whimpering. They were gone forever, and she would be a sad, floppy puddle for always."

### Beat 3.5: Recovery
Text:
> Rocking slightly, Liska pulled her fluffy tail to her chest, hugging it tightly with both paws. The familiar feeling helped the scary feeling shrink just a little. Through her fear, she heard a new sound from above: a warm, booming laugh.

Review:
> **Critique:** The transition from her internal coping to hearing the external cue is slightly coincidental as written. The causal link can be strengthened.
>
> **Suggestion:** Make the connection more explicit. Her self-soothing action should be what allows the external world to penetrate her bubble of fear. "Rocking slightly, she hugged her fluffy tail to her chest. The familiar softness helped quiet the loud thumping in her ears *just* enough. And in that tiny moment of quiet, she heard a sound from above: a warm, booming laugh."

### Beat 3.6: Re-evaluation
Text:
> The kind voice made her pause. She knew that laugh! It was the Kebab-Man. Like a sunbeam, she remembered the rule: find a helper you know, or one working at a stall. The Kebab-Man was both!

Review:
> **Critique:** The use of the `Sunbeam (Солнечный Зайчик)` metaphor, which is primarily associated with joy, may not be the most precise fit here. The core emotional experience is one of cognitive clarity and dawning hope, not pure happiness.
>
> **Suggestion:** Reframe the metaphor to emphasize its function as illumination. "The kind voice made her pause... The rule she had forgotten suddenly shone in her mind like a warm sunbeam, lighting up a clear path out of the scary darkness." This maintains the positive valence of the metaphor while aligning it with the function of a solution appearing in a state of confusion.

## Act 4: Climax & Solution – Overcoming Externally & Internally
### Beat 4.1: Final Attempt / Approach (Applying New Understanding / Changed Behaviour)
Text:
> Liska crawls out from under the counter and tugs on the Kebab Man's apron. This is her applying the new solution: asking for help.
> - Slot: `Coping Strategy`: `My "I Need Help" Plan`

Review:
> Good. This is the direct physical manifestation of her internal decision in Beat 3.5. It's a clear, brave action that applies the `My "I Need Help" Plan` coping strategy.

### Beat 4.2: Rising Tension / Suspense
Text:
> The Kebab Man immediately stopped, his presence warm and reassuring as he knelt down. He introduced the approaching Guard as a trusted friend and an expert at finding families. Then he rang a loud brass bell, signaling the "chain of help" was in motion.

Review:
> **Critique:** The text states that the Kebab Man introduces the Guard, but the dialogue that models this crucial `Social Navigation Support` is missing. This is a "tell, don't show" moment.
>
> **Suggestion:** Add the explicit dialogue to model the trust transfer. Kebab Man: "Ah, just the expert we need! Liska, this is my friend, Guard Omar. He is the very best at finding lost little ones."

### Beat 4.3: The Turning Point / Final Push (Demonstrating Growth in Action)
Text:
> The Guard knelt. "And who are we looking for?" he asked calmly. Liska described her mama's purple dress and her papa being "really, really big." The Guard nodded. "A clear description. But from down here, all I see are knees. With your permission, you shall be my lookout."

Review:
> **Critique:** The Guard's immediate jump to problem-solving ("And who are we looking for?") could be perceived as abrupt by a child who is still emotionally fragile. A moment of rapport-building is needed first.
>
> **Suggestion:** Add a beat of validation before the question. The Guard kneels. He doesn't speak at first, just offers a calm, quiet presence. Then, very gently: "Hello there. That was a very brave thing you just did. Now, I have a feeling you are looking for someone important." This validates her action and acknowledges her emotional state before moving into the solution.

### Beat 4.4: Success (External Problem Resolved)
Text:
> Lifted above the crowd on the Guard's shoulder, Liska can see over everyone's heads. She spots her parents, who are looking frantically for her. "There they are!"

Review:
> The resolution is a direct result of the empowered action in the previous beat. This provides a clear and satisfying causal link for the child reader: "Because I became the lookout, I could find my parents."

### Beat 4.5: Immediate Reward / Relief (Accomplishment & Positive Emotion)
Text:
> "Mama! Papa!" Liska cried as the Guard marched her over. Mara and Daric swept her into a giant, relieved hug that smelled like home. The Guard tipped his hat. "Your lookout has completed her duties. A job very well done."

Review:
> **Critique:** The praise is perfect (`Process Praise`), and the conclusion of the Guard's role is effective. It could be strengthened with a small, personal gesture to make the interaction more memorable and warm.
>
> **Suggestion:** Add a final, small gesture from the Guard that reinforces the positive relationship. "The Guard tipped his hat... He then gave her a slow, formal wink." The wink adds a touch of warmth and shared secret to his formal demeanor, making him more endearing.

## Act 5: Resolution & Conclusion – Integrating the Growth
### Beat 5.1: Relaxation / Immediate Aftermath (Processing the Journey)
Text:
> The reunited family goes together to the Kebab Man's stall to thank both helpers. They buy kebabs for everyone. The mood is relieved and happy.

Review:
> Modeling gratitude by thanking the helpers is an important pro-social lesson. The shared meal provides a moment of calm connection and brings the story full circle back to the initial temptation, but this time it is fulfilled within the safety of the family unit.

### Beat 5.2: Reflection & Learning (Internalizing the Lesson & Growth)
Text:
> As they walked from the stall, Mara held Liska's paw tightly. "You were so brave to ask for help," she whispered. "That was exactly the right thing to do."

Review:
> **Critique:** The reinforcement from the parent is excellent. To fully close the emotional loop according to `Emotion Validation` guidelines, it should also acknowledge the negative feeling that preceded the brave action.
>
> **Suggestion:** Add validation of the fear. "As they walked... Mara held Liska's paw tightly. 'It must have been so scary when you were lost,' she whispered. 'And you were so brave to ask for help. That was exactly the right thing to do.'" Acknowledging the negative feeling first makes the praise for the subsequent action even more powerful.

### Beat 5.3: Making it Right / Integration (Acknowledging & Embodying Change)
Text:
> Liska looked up at her mother and squeezed her paw back. It was a silent promise to remember the rule, and to try her best to stay close.

Review:
> **Critique:** "It was a silent promise" is a narrator's interpretation. This can be shown more actively from the character's perspective.
>
> **Suggestion:** Frame the action as the promise itself. "Liska looked up at her mother and squeezed her paw back—a tight, quick squeeze that said, *I will.*" This makes the moment more active and character-driven.

Text:
> Safely nestled between her parents, Liska took a happy bite of her kebab. She decided that finding helpers was a very good plan. But holding Papa's hand, she thought, was an even better one.

Review:
> **Critique:** None. This is a perfect conclusion. It shows the child has internalized the practical lesson in her own terms, while reaffirming that the primary goal is emotional and physical safety with her attachment figures. It elegantly balances the story's theme of growing independence with the core need for connection.

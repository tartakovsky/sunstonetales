### Arguments For/Against NPH

**Your Case FOR:**
- Children need explicit scaffolding to build generalizable skills
- Without naming the pattern, children only learn specific responses, not the transferable framework
- Adults who learn NPH can successfully apply it, but don't develop it naturally
- Most adults fail at emotional regulation without explicit teaching
- Children can't learn the framework cognitively, but they can learn the behaviors from the examples. What we need to do to turn it into a framework eventually is just subtle hints that it is not a random set of behaviors here and there.

**My Challenges:**
- Abstract concepts may be too complex for 4-6 year olds
- "Pause" could confuse children (pause what? feelings? actions?)
- Gap between understanding concepts in calm story moments vs. applying when dysregulated
- Untested whether children actually use this framework in real situations

### Strategies to Make NPH Stick
1. **Game mechanics** (Shadow Detective, Explorer's Rhythm, Shadow Theater)
2. **Songs/chants with rhythm** ("Feel the beat-beat-beat...")
3. **Natural process praise** ("You noticed your drums, paused with Slothy, and helped yourself!")
4. **Physical movements** tied to each step
5. **Consistent modeling across multiple stories**

### Scaffolding Variations
- Child initiates one part: "Mom, I noticed my heart beats fast!"
- Parent completes sequence: guides through pause and help
- Gradual release: "I'm scared, I hugged my sloth, but now what?"
- Builds autonomy over time

### Critical Story Elements
- **Universality message**: Same three steps work everywhere - you can notice/pause/help differently, but the sequence remains
- **First attempt**: Mother does guided exploration (must stay long enough - avoid seeming neglectful)
- **Shadow play**: Use only to practice established tools, not as separate intervention

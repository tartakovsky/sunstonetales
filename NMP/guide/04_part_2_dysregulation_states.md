# Part 3: Reading Your Child's Emotional State

## 3.1. What You're Seeing and What to Do

Dysregulation doesn't look the same every time. Your child's emotional state changes based on how overwhelmed they are, by what, and how they express it.

Your response needs to match what you're seeing. This section teaches you to read the four different dysregulation states and adapt your approach.

---

## 3.2 The Science

Research shows children express dysregulation in two main ways:

**Internalizing:** Distress directed inward (seeking comfort, withdrawing).
**Externalizing:** Distress directed outward (aggression, physical discharge).

Within these, the intensity and target of the behavior vary.

Understanding which state you're seeing helps you choose the right response.

---

## 3.3 The 4 Dysregulation States

### State 1: Seeking Support (Internalizing)

**What it looks like:**

**High Intensity:**
- Crying intensely, can't catch breath.
- Clinging to you, won't let go.
- Running TO you for help.
- Terror, panic, overwhelming sadness.
- Cannot talk or form words.

**Moderate Intensity:**
- Upset but can talk (even if their voice shakes).
- Seeking you out, approaching you.
- Verbal about what's wrong ("I'm scared" / "That hurt my feelings").
- Sad, worried, or frustrated, but still somewhat in control.

**What's happening in their brain:**
Emotional overwhelm. Their internal distress is too big to handle alone. They're seeking your nervous system to help regulate theirs.

**What to do:**

**For High Intensity:**
1.  **Get close immediately** (pick them up if they are young enough, kneel down if not).
2.  **Use physical contact** (a hug, a hold, rocking, a hand on the back, whatever they accept).
3.  **Maintain a calm presence** (slow breathing, soft voice, steady).
4.  **Validate while regulating:** "You're so scared. It's okay. I've got you." / "You're really sad. I'm here."
5.  **Stay until their breathing slows** (this can take 2-20 minutes, sometimes longer).

**For Moderate Intensity:**
1.  **Get to eye level.**
2.  **Validate the emotion:** "You're really frustrated about this." / "That does feel disappointing."
3.  **Stay physically close** (sit nearby, put a hand on their shoulder).
4.  **Support their coping tools:** "Do you want to try your deep breaths?" / "Would a hug help?"
5.  **Listen Actively** (let them talk it out if they want).

**Don't:** Dismiss ("It's not a big deal"), rush to fix it, or leave them alone to "calm down."

**Why this works:** They're asking for help regulating. Your calm presence and validation allow their nervous system to return to baseline.

---

### State 2: Shutting Down (Internalizing, Freeze)

**What it looks like:**
- A blank stare, unresponsive to questions.
- Won't talk or make eye contact.
- Sitting motionless, hiding, or curled up.
- Not crying, not angry, just... gone.
- They may look "calm," but they're actually overwhelmed.

**What's happening in their brain:**
The nervous system has shut down. This is the **freeze response**; when fight and flight aren't options, the brain protects itself by shutting down. This is an overwhelm that looks quiet but isn't.

**What to do:**
1.  **Sit nearby** (close enough that they know you're there)
2.  **Offer physical contact**: Try hugging them, touching softly. BUT: stop immediately if they withdraw, for some children touch during shutdown may be overwhelming. 
2.  **Use soft, quiet words** (very few): "I'm here when you're ready." / "You're safe."
3.  **Make no demands** (don't ask questions or require eye contact or responses).
4.  **Wait** (this could be 5 minutes or 45 minutes; stay patient).
5.  **Notice signs that they're emerging** (slight movement, looking around, eye contact).
6.  **Offer gentle connection when they are ready:** "Hi. I'm here. Do you want to sit together?"

**Don't:** Force them to talk, interpret silence as them being "fine now," demand explanations, or rush them.

**Why this works:** Shutdown is a freeze response to unbearable overwhelm. Your non-demanding, safe presence allows their nervous system to slowly come back online without added pressure.

### State 3: Aggression Directed at Objects (Externalizing, Undirected)

**What it looks like:**
- Throwing toys, books, or objects (not AT people).
- Hitting furniture, walls, or the floor.
- Screaming, flailing, kicking at objects.
- A physical discharge of overwhelm.
- NOT seeking you out.
- NOT directing aggression at people, just explosive, reactive behavior.

**What's happening in their brain:**
An overwhelmed nervous system trying to discharge intensity through physical action. This is **reactive aggression**, an impulsive, emotional response to frustration. It is not planned or goal-directed.

**What to do:**
1.  **Ensure safety first** (move breakables, protect siblings, remove dangerous objects).
2.  **Set a boundary if needed** (calm, firm, one sentence): "I can't let you throw that. I'm moving it here."
3.  **Get close when it's safe** (kneel nearby, use a soft voice).
4.  **Offer physical contact**: spread your arms, invite them to hug. Put hand on their shoulder softly. But do not force it if they don't want it.
5.  **Validate the emotion:** "You're SO angry right now. Your body feels like it needs to move."
6.  **Offer a calm presence** (stay close, breathe slowly, wait).
7.  **After they calm down, investigate the trigger** (5+ minutes later): "What happened before you got so upset?"

**Don't:** Yell, match their intensity, give consequences during the meltdown, or physically restrain unless safety requires it.

**Why this works:** Physical discharge helps some children regulate. Your boundary creates safety. Your calm and validation teach them that their intensity is manageable.

---

### State 4: Aggression Directed at People (Externalizing, Targeted)

**What it looks like:**
- Hitting YOU, siblings, or other people.
- Kicking, biting, or scratching someone.
- Yelling hurtful words AT a specific person.
- The aggression is targeted at a person, not objects.

**What's happening in their brain:**
Full amygdala activation. This is **reactive aggression**, which is impulsive and emotionally driven. They're overwhelmed, and the person (you, a sibling, a peer) has become associated with their frustration. It is not planned or calculated. Their brain is in survival mode.

**What to do:**

**1. Protect immediately**
- Block hits calmly (catch their hand, step back).
- DO NOT hit back or yell.
- For sibling aggression, move between the children physically.
- Use a calm, firm voice: "I can't let you hurt me/them."

**2. Create brief space if needed for safety** (30 seconds - 2 minutes MAX)
- If aggression continues despite the boundary: "I'm moving back so we're both safe."
- Stay in visual contact (across the room, in the doorway, nearby).
- YOU stay calm (take deep breaths, drop your shoulders).
- Watch for the aggression to stop.

**3. Return with a calm presence AS SOON AS the aggression stops** (within 2-5 minutes)
- Do NOT leave them alone to "figure it out."
- Reconnect quickly: "The hitting stopped. I'm coming back close now."
- Get close again (kneel, sit nearby).

**4. Regulate together** (2-10 minutes)
- Use a soft voice: "You were very angry."
- "It's okay to feel angry. It's not okay to hit people."
- Validate the emotion and set a boundary on the behavior.
- Offer physical comfort if they'll accept it (a hug, a hand on the back).
- Stay until their breathing slows.

**5. After they are FULLY calm, address what happened** (15+ minutes later)
- "What was happening that made you so mad?"
- "When you feel that angry again, what could you do instead of hitting?"
- For sibling conflicts, both children need support (the victim gets comfort first, the aggressor gets co-regulation).

**Don't:**
- Leave them alone for long periods.
- Yell, threaten, or hit back.
- Demand immediate apologies mid-dysregulation.
- Lecture while they're still escalated.
- Use the space as punishment ("Go think about what you did").

**Why this works:**
- Brief space creates immediate safety for both people (it is NOT abandonment when it's brief).
- Your quick return communicates, "I won't leave you alone in your overwhelm."
- Staying calm models the regulation they need to learn.
- Validating the feeling with a clear boundary teaches them that emotions are okay, but hurting people is not.

<!-- Critical Distinction Box: -->

#### Brief Safety Space vs. Punitive Time-Out

**❌ What DOESN'T work (Punitive):**
- "Go to your room until you can behave."
- Leaving them alone for 10+ minutes.
- Withdrawal of connection as punishment.
- Ignoring them completely.

**✅ What DOES work (Co-regulation with space):**
- "I'm creating space so neither of us gets hurt."
- Staying visible, staying calm.
- Returning within 2-5 minutes (as soon as hitting stops).
- Reconnecting to provide regulation support.

**The key:** Space is temporary and for safety only. You return QUICKLY to co-regulate. The child is never left alone to manage overwhelming emotion.

## 3.5 Quick Reference Table
| **State** | **What You See** | **Your Response** |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Seeking Support** | Crying, clinging, seeking you out | Full co-regulation: presence, physical comfort, and validation. |
| **Shutting Down** | Withdrawn, blank, unresponsive, frozen | Quiet presence nearby → no pressure → wait → gentle reconnection. |
| **Aggression At Objects** | Throwing/hitting objects, screaming (not AT people) | Safety → boundary if needed → calm presence → validate. |
| **Aggression At People** | Hitting/yelling AT you, siblings, others | Boundary → brief space if unsafe → return quickly (2-5 min) → co-regulate. |

## 3.6 Remember
- Aggression at people requires a boundary, brief space, and a QUICK return.
- The same child can show different states at different times.
- Younger children (3-4) show more undirected aggression and seeking support.
- Older children (6-7) may show more shutting down or person-directed aggression.
- Read what you're seeing so you can adapt.

## 3.7 Scientific Basis
- Internalizing/Externalizing: Achenbach (1966, 1978)
- Reactive aggression: Dodge & Coie (1987), Dodge (1991)
- Shutdown/Freeze: Perry (Neurosequential Model), autism/trauma literature
- Time-out + reconnection: PCIT research, Dadds & Tully (2019), evidence-based parent training programs

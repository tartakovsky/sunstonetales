# 1. When Your Child Is Dysregulated Right Now

Your child is screaming, hitting, throwing things, or melting down. This is called dysregulation. You need to know what to do in the next 60 seconds.

<!-- Weak -->
This is the protocol. It works because it matches how their brain actually functions.

---

## 1.1 The 3 R's: Regulate, Relate, Reason

---

### Step 1: Regulate Yourself First
**Time: 30 seconds**

Before you do anything else, calm your own nervous system. A child cannot self-regulate (calm down on their own or control their emotions). They do it through your calm. A dysregulated adult cannot regulate a dysregulated child.

**Do this:**
- Take 3 slow breaths (in through nose, out through mouth).
- Drop your shoulders.
- Lower your voice tone (even if you haven't spoken yet).
- Think: *"Their brain can't handle this alone yet. They need me to be calm to calm down. This is biology, not behavior."*

**Why this matters:** Your child's nervous system reads YOUR nervous system. If you're escalated, they stay escalated. Your calm is the first step.

---

### Step 2: Regulate and Relate Together
**Time: 1-10 minutes (sometimes longer, which is normal)**

Approach your child. Provide a calm presence AND validate their emotion at the same time. These happen together, not in sequence.

**Physical presence (Regulate):**
- Get to their eye level (kneel, sit).
- Move close (within arm's reach).
- Offer touch if they accept it (a hug, a hand on the back, holding a hand).
- If they pull away, stay nearby without touching; don't force contact.
- Use a soft, slow voice tone.

**Words (Relate/Validate):**
- **Name the feeling:** "You're so angry." / "That's really scary." / "You're frustrated."
- **Make it okay:** "It's okay to feel that." / "Big feelings are hard."
- **Stay simple:** "I'm here." / "You're safe." / "It is going to be fine soon."

**Examples of Regulate + Relate together:**
- Child runs to you crying → You kneel and hug them → "You're really scared. It's okay. I've got you."
- Child throwing toys → You sit nearby → "You're so frustrated right now. That's really hard."

<!-- This needs improvement -->
- Child hits you → You create space → "You're very angry. I won't let you hit me, and I'm staying close."

**DO NOT (yet):**
- Ask "why" questions.
- Lecture or explain rules.
- Give consequences.
- Problem-solve.
- Demand apologies or eye contact.

**Why:** Their prefrontal cortex (the logic brain) is offline. They cannot process reasoning. They need your calm and validation to help their nervous system return to baseline.

---

### Step 3: Reason (When They're Calmer)
**Time: 5+ minutes after meltdown starts (sometimes 30+ minutes)**

**Wait for these signs that they're ready:**
- Breathing has slowed.
- Crying is less intense or has stopped.
- They can make eye contact.
- They respond to simple questions.

**NOW you can reason:**
- Ask what happened: "Can you tell me what was hard?"
- Problem-solve together: "What could help next time?"
- Offer choices: "Do you need more hug time or some space?"
- Set boundaries if needed: "You can feel angry. You cannot hit people."
- Repair if needed: "I yelled earlier. That wasn't okay. I'm sorry."

**Why:** Their higher brain (logic, problem-solving) is back online. Now they can learn.

---

<!-- Bottom of page, highlighted box: -->

## 1.2 Key Points
- **Regulate and Relate** happen together (calm presence and emotion validation).
- **Reason** happens after (when they're calm enough to think).
- Regulation time varies wildly, from 2 minutes to 2 hours (both are normal).
- Younger children mean longer regulation time.
- Your presence is the tool, not your words.

# 2. Your Child's Brain Can't Self-Regulate Yet (And That's Normal)

You're not failing. Your child isn't broken. Their brain is still building the parts that manage big emotions.

Understanding why co-regulation works will help you stay consistent when it's hard.

---

## 2.1 Brain Development Timeline

**The part of the brain that handles emotional regulation doesn't finish developing until the mid-20s.**

Here's what's happening in your 3-7 year old:

**The Amygdala (Emotion Center):**
- Fully online and highly active.
- Detects threats (real or perceived).
- Triggers big emotional responses.
- Develops FASTER than the regulation centers.

**The Prefrontal Cortex (Logic & Self-Control Center):**
- Still under construction; it won't mature until the mid-20s.
- Responsible for planning, impulse control, emotional regulation, and logical thinking.
- In ages 3-7, it is building connections but is NOT fully functional yet.
- Goes completely OFFLINE during stress.

**What this means:**
Your child has a fully operational emotion-generator (amygdala) but an underdeveloped emotion-manager (prefrontal cortex).

**Result:** Big emotions with no built-in management system.

This is why they melt down over "small" things. To their brain, it's not small; it's overwhelming, and they have no internal capacity to handle it alone.

## 2.2 What Co-Regulation Actually Is

**Co-regulation** is when your calm nervous system helps regulate their developing one.

This isn't a parenting style. It's biology.

**How it works:**
- Your child's nervous system is immature.
- During dysregulation, their stress response (fight/flight/freeze) activates.
- They cannot calm themselves; their brain literally doesn't have the wiring yet.
- Your calm, regulated nervous system provides the external regulation they need.
- Over thousands of repetitions, they internalize this capacity.
- Eventually, they develop self-regulation (around ages 7-12, continuing into adulthood).

**The developmental path:**
- **Ages 0-3:** Full co-regulation (you do all the regulating).
- **Ages 3-5:** Heavy scaffolding (you're there, and they're starting to practice).
- **Ages 5-7:** Coaching (they can sometimes regulate with your support).
- **Ages 7+:** Guided independence (they're building self-regulation but still need backup).

**Research evidence:**
Attachment science shows that **responsive caregiving** (staying calm, validating emotions, providing comfort) leads to:
- Secure attachment.
- Better emotional regulation in childhood.
- Healthier stress responses in adulthood.
- The ability to form healthy relationships.

Children who receive co-regulation learn: *"Emotions are manageable. I can handle this. Adults will help me."*

Children who DON'T receive co-regulation learn: *"Emotions are emergencies. I'm alone in this. I must hide what I feel."*

---

## 2.3 Why Punishment Doesn't Work

When you punish a dysregulated child, here's what happens in their brain:

**1. The Amygdala activates HARDER** (punishment = threat).
- The fear response intensifies.
- Stress hormones flood the system.
- The fight/flight/freeze response deepens.

**2. The Prefrontal cortex shuts down COMPLETELY.**
- They cannot access logic.
- They cannot learn the lesson you're trying to teach.
- They cannot rationally connect behavior to a consequence.

**3. They learn the wrong lesson:**
- ❌ NOT: "I shouldn't hit when I'm angry."
- ✅ INSTEAD: "My emotions are dangerous and I must hide them."
- ✅ ALSO: "Coming to an adult makes it worse; I can't trust them."

**What punishment actually teaches:**
- Compliance through fear, not self-regulation.
- Emotional suppression, not emotional management.
- Adult authority is based on power, not on earned respect.
- Adults are a source of danger to avoid, not a source of help to approach.

**The long-term result:**
Adults who can't regulate their internal reality. They never learned how; they only learned to hide it.

---

**Co-regulation teaches actual regulation:**
- ✅ "My emotions are signals, not emergencies."
- ✅ "I can handle big feelings with support."
- ✅ "Adults help me when I'm overwhelmed. I can trust them. I can open up."
- ✅ Eventually: "I can manage this myself."

---

## 2.4 What You're Building Over Time

Co-regulation is temporary. You're teaching them to do this themselves.

**The progression:**

**Ages 3-4:**
- You provide full regulation support.
- They're learning that emotions have names, feelings pass, and adults stay calm.

**Ages 5-6:**
- You're scaffolding (present, but they're practicing).
- They're learning to identify "I'm angry," use simple coping tools, and ask for help.

**Ages 6-7:**
- You're coaching (they can do it, but you're their backup).
- They're learning self-soothing strategies, problem-solving, and emotional recovery.

**Ages 7+:**
- They're building true self-regulation.
- You're still there when it's too big, but they can handle more on their own.

---

**This process takes YEARS. That's not a flaw; it's development.**

Your job isn't to make them self-regulate at age 4. Your job is to co-regulate so consistently that by age 10, they've internalized the capacity.

- Self-regulation develops FROM co-regulation, not instead of it.
- You're not creating dependency; you're building capacity.
- The time you invest now equals emotional health for life.

# 3. What You're Actually Seeing and What to Do

Dysregulation doesn't look the same every time. Your child's emotional state changes based on how overwhelmed they are and how they express it.

Your response needs to match what you're seeing. This section teaches you to read the four different dysregulation states and adapt your approach.

---

## 3.1 The Science

Research shows children express dysregulation in two main ways:

**Internalizing:** Distress directed inward (seeking comfort, withdrawing).
**Externalizing:** Distress directed outward (aggression, physical discharge).

Within these, the intensity and target of the behavior vary.

Understanding which state you're seeing helps you choose the right response.

---

## 3.2 The 4 Dysregulation States

### State 1: Distress Seeking (Internalizing)

**What it looks like:**

**High Intensity:**
- Crying intensely, can't catch breath.
- Clinging to you, won't let go.
- Running TO you for help.
- Terror, panic, overwhelming sadness.
- Cannot talk or form words.

**Moderate Intensity:**
- Upset but can talk (even if their voice shakes).
- Seeking you out, approaching you.
- Verbal about what's wrong ("I'm scared" / "That hurt my feelings").
- Sad, worried, or frustrated, but still somewhat in control.

**What's happening in their brain:**
Emotional overwhelm. Their internal distress is too big to handle alone. They're seeking your nervous system to help regulate theirs.

**What to do:**

**For High Intensity:**
1.  **Get close immediately** (pick them up if they are young enough, kneel down if not).
2.  **Use physical contact** (a hug, a hold, rocking, a hand on the back, whatever they accept).
3.  **Maintain a calm presence** (slow breathing, soft voice, steady).
4.  **Validate while regulating:** "You're so scared. It's okay. I've got you." / "You're really sad. I'm here."
5.  **Stay until their breathing slows** (this can take 2-20 minutes, sometimes longer).

**For Moderate Intensity:**
1.  **Get to eye level.**
2.  **Validate the emotion:** "You're really frustrated about this." / "That does feel disappointing."
3.  **Stay physically close** (sit nearby, put a hand on their shoulder).
4.  **Support their coping tools:** "Do you want to try your deep breaths?" / "Would a hug help?"
5.  **Listen** (let them talk it out if they want).

**Don't:** Dismiss ("It's not a big deal"), rush to fix it, or leave them alone to "calm down."

**Why this works:** They're asking for help regulating. Your calm presence and validation allow their nervous system to return to baseline.

---

### State 2: Aggression Directed at Objects/Environment (Externalizing, Undirected)

**What it looks like:**
- Throwing toys, books, or objects (not AT people).
- Hitting furniture, walls, or the floor.
- Screaming, flailing, kicking at objects.
- A physical discharge of overwhelm.
- NOT seeking you out.
- NOT directing aggression at people, just explosive, reactive behavior.

**What's happening in their brain:**
An overwhelmed nervous system trying to discharge intensity through physical action. This is **reactive aggression**, an impulsive, emotional response to frustration. It is not planned or goal-directed.

**What to do:**
1.  **Ensure safety first** (move breakables, protect siblings, remove dangerous objects).
2.  **Set a boundary if needed** (calm, firm, one sentence): "I can't let you throw that. I'm moving it here."
3.  **Get close when it's safe** (kneel nearby, use a soft voice).
4.  **Validate the emotion:** "You're SO angry right now. Your body feels like it needs to move."
5.  **Offer a calm presence** (stay close, breathe slowly, wait).
6.  **After they calm down, investigate the trigger** (5+ minutes later): "What happened before you got so upset?"

**Don't:** Yell, match their intensity, give consequences during the meltdown, or physically restrain unless safety requires it.

**Why this works:** Physical discharge helps some children regulate. Your boundary creates safety. Your calm and validation teach them that their intensity is manageable.

---

### State 3: Aggression Directed at People (Externalizing, Targeted)

**What it looks like:**
- Hitting YOU, siblings, or other people.
- Kicking, biting, or scratching someone.
- Yelling hurtful words AT a specific person.
- The aggression is targeted at a person, not objects.

**What's happening in their brain:**
Full amygdala activation. This is **reactive aggression**, which is impulsive and emotionally driven. They're overwhelmed, and the person (you, a sibling, a peer) has become associated with their frustration. It is not planned or calculated. Their brain is in survival mode.

**What to do:**

**1. Protect immediately** (5-10 seconds)
- Block hits calmly (catch their hand, step back).
- DO NOT hit back or yell.
- For sibling aggression, move between the children physically.
- Use a calm, firm voice: "I can't let you hurt [me/them]."

**2. Create brief space if needed for safety** (30 seconds - 2 minutes MAX)
- If aggression continues despite the boundary: "I'm moving back so we're both safe."
- Stay in visual contact (across the room, in the doorway, nearby).
- YOU stay calm (take deep breaths, drop your shoulders).
- Watch for the aggression to stop.

**3. Return with a calm presence AS SOON AS the aggression stops** (within 2-5 minutes)
- Do NOT leave them alone to "figure it out."
- Reconnect quickly: "The hitting stopped. I'm coming back close now."
- Get close again (kneel, sit nearby).

**4. Regulate together** (2-10 minutes)
- Use a soft voice: "You were very angry."
- "It's okay to feel angry. It's not okay to hit people."
- Validate the emotion and set a boundary on the behavior.
- Offer physical comfort if they'll accept it (a hug, a hand on the back).
- Stay until their breathing slows.

**5. After they are FULLY calm, address what happened** (15+ minutes later)
- "What was happening that made you so mad?"
- "When you feel that angry again, what could you do instead of hitting?"
- For sibling conflicts, both children need support (the victim gets comfort first, the aggressor gets co-regulation).

**Don't:**
- Leave them alone for long periods.
- Yell, threaten, or hit back.
- Demand immediate apologies mid-dysregulation.
- Lecture while they're still escalated.
- Use the space as punishment ("Go think about what you did").

**Why this works:**
- Brief space creates immediate safety for both people (it is NOT abandonment).
- Your quick return communicates, "I won't leave you alone in your overwhelm."
- Staying calm models the regulation they need to learn.
- Validating the feeling with a clear boundary teaches them that emotions are okay, but hurting people is not.

---

## 3.3 Brief Safety Space vs. Punitive Time-Out

**❌ What DOESN'T work (Punitive):**
- "Go to your room until you can behave."
- Leaving them alone for 10+ minutes.
- Withdrawal of connection as punishment.
- Ignoring them completely.

**✅ What DOES work (Co-regulation with space):**
- "I'm creating space so neither of us gets hurt."
- Staying visible, staying calm.
- Returning within 2-5 minutes (as soon as hitting stops).
- Reconnecting to provide regulation support.

**The key:** Space is temporary and for safety only. You return QUICKLY to co-regulate. The child is never left alone to manage overwhelming emotion.

---

### State 4: Shutdown (Internalizing, Freeze)

**What it looks like:**
- A blank stare, unresponsive to questions.
- Won't talk or make eye contact.
- Sitting motionless, hiding, or curled up.
- Not crying, not angry, just... gone.
- They may look "calm," but they're actually overwhelmed.

**What's happening in their brain:**
The nervous system has shut down. This is the **freeze response**; when fight and flight aren't options, the brain protects itself by shutting down. This is an overwhelm that looks quiet but isn't.

**What to do:**
1.  **Sit nearby** (close enough that they know you're there, but not touching unless they initiate).
2.  **Use soft, quiet words** (very few): "I'm here when you're ready." / "You're safe."
3.  **Make no demands** (don't ask questions or require eye contact or responses).
4.  **Wait** (this could be 5 minutes or 45 minutes; stay patient).
5.  **Notice signs that they're emerging** (slight movement, looking around, eye contact).
6.  **Offer gentle connection when they are ready:** "Hi. I'm here. Do you want to sit together?"

**Don't:** Force them to talk, interpret silence as them being "fine now," demand explanations, or rush them.

**Why this works:** Shutdown is a freeze response to unbearable overwhelm. Your non-demanding, safe presence allows their nervous system to slowly come back online without added pressure.

---

## 3.4 Quick Reference Table

| **State** | **What You See** | **Scientific Category** | **Your Response** |
|-----------|------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Distress Seeking** | Crying, clinging, seeking you out | Internalizing behavior | Full co-regulation: presence, physical comfort, and validation. |
| **Aggression At Objects** | Throwing/hitting objects, screaming (not AT people) | Externalizing + Reactive (undirected) | Safety → boundary if needed → calm presence → validate. |
| **Aggression At People** | Hitting/yelling AT you, siblings, others | Externalizing + Reactive (person-directed) | Boundary → brief space if unsafe → return quickly (2-5 min) → co-regulate. |
| **Shutdown** | Withdrawn, blank, unresponsive, frozen | Internalizing + Freeze response | Quiet presence nearby → no pressure → wait → gentle reconnection. |

- The same child can show different states at different times.
- Younger children (3-4) show more undirected aggression and distress seeking.
- Older children (6-7) may show more shutdown or person-directed aggression.

# 4. The Moves That Actually Work

These are the specific actions you take during co-regulation. They're not scripts to memorize; they're tools you adapt to your child's needs.

**Start simple:** Pick 2-3 tools. Practice those until they feel natural, then add more.

**Foundation:** Your own calm is required for ALL of these tools to work. If you're dysregulated, regulate yourself first (3 deep breaths, drop your shoulders, slow your voice, and remember: they are not your enemy, they just can't deal with emotions yet and need help).

---

## 4.1 Core Regulation Tools
*Use these during any dysregulation state*

---

### Calm Presence
- **What it is:** Staying physically close with a regulated nervous system.
- **When:** ALL states, as this is the foundation.
- **How:** Breathe slowly, soften your face, lower your shoulders, and stay within arm's reach.
- **Example:** Your child is melting down → You sit on the floor nearby, breathing calmly, and saying nothing yet.

### Physical Contact
- **What it is:** Touch that soothes and regulates.
- **When:** Distress seeking (always), aggression (after it stops), shutdown (only if they initiate).
- **How:** A hug, a hand on the back, holding a hand, rocking together; whatever they accept.
- **Example:** "Would a hug help?" / Simply opening your arms if they approach.
- **Note:** Some children pull away when upset. Respect that and stay close without touching.

### Intentional Connection
- **What it is:** Getting to their level with a soft voice and gentle approach.
- **When:** At the beginning of ANY co-regulation.
- **How:** Kneel or sit so you're at eye level, speak softly and slowly, and move toward them gently.
- **Example:** Child is crying → You kneel down and say in a soft voice, "Hey. I'm here."

### Emotion Acknowledgment
- **What it is:** Naming what you observe without judgment.
- **When:** After you're close, while regulating.
- **How:** State what you see: "You look really angry." / "You seem scared."
- **Example:** They are throwing toys → "You're so frustrated right now."

### Feeling Validation
- **What it is:** Making emotions okay to have.
- **When:** During the Regulate and Relate phase (Steps 1-2 of the 3 R's).
- **How:** "It's okay to feel [emotion]." / "Big feelings are hard." / "That makes sense."
- **Example:** "It's okay to be mad that your tower fell down. You worked hard on that."

### Active Listening
- **What it is:** Giving your full attention with minimal words, reflecting back what they say.
- **When:** After they're calmer and want to talk.
- **How:** Nod, say "mm-hmm," and repeat their words back, and maintain eye contact.
- **Example:** Child: "She took my toy!" → You: "She took your toy. That felt unfair."

---

## 4.2 Building Tools
*Use these as you get comfortable with the core tools*

---

### Gentle Narration
- **What it is:** Explaining what's happening to reduce uncertainty.
- **When:** During distress, during transitions, or before potentially scary things.
- **How:** Use a calm voice and simple words: "First we'll [X], then [Y]."
- **Example:** "The doctor will look in your ears. It won't hurt, it just feels weird."

### Process Praise
- **What it is:** Acknowledging effort or strategy, not the outcome.
- **When:** AFTER regulation, during calm moments, or when catching positive behavior.
- **How:** Name what they DID: "You kept trying different ways." / "You used your words."
- **Example:** NOT "Good job!" → INSTEAD "You asked for help instead of hitting. That took work."

### Choice Offering
- **What it is:** Giving small, real choices to restore a sense of control.
- **When:** During moderate distress, after aggression stops, or during emergence from shutdown.
- **How:** Offer two options (both acceptable to you): "Do you want [X] or [Y]?"
- **Example:** "Do you want to sit on my lap or next to me?" / "Hug or space?"

### Mistake Normalizing
- **What it is:** Framing errors as learning opportunities, not failures.
- **When:** After they are calm, when addressing what happened.
- **How:** "Mistakes help us learn." / "Everyone has hard moments."
- **Example:** "You hit your sister. That's not okay, AND you were really overwhelmed. Let's figure out what to do next time."

### Patient Waiting
- **What it is:** Giving them time to process without rushing.
- **When:** During shutdown, when they need to think, or when they're almost calm.
- **How:** Silence. Presence. No hurry. Let them set the pace.
- **Example:** A child is staring at the floor → You sit quietly nearby for 5+ minutes without speaking.

### Modeling Calm
- **What it is:** Demonstrating regulated breathing and peaceful energy.
- **When:** Throughout ALL dysregulation.
- **How:** Slow your breathing visibly, relax your body, and speak in slow, low tones.
- **Example:** "I'm going to take a slow breath. You can try it with me if you want."

---

## 4.3 Advanced Tools
*Add these when you're solid with the core tools*

---

### Gentle Reminders
- **What it is:** Helping them remember coping strategies they know.
- **When:** During moderate distress, when they're starting to calm down.
- **How:** Use a soft voice and a question format: "What helped last time you felt this way?"
- **Example:** "Remember your deep breathing? Want to try that?"

### Partnership Language
- **What it is:** Using "we" to show you're on the same team.
- **When:** After they are calm, during problem-solving.
- **How:** "We can figure this out together." / "Let's see what helps."
- **Example:** NOT "You need to stop crying" → "We'll work through this feeling together."

### Boundary Holding
- **What it is:** Setting clear, kind limits while still offering emotional support.
- **When:** During aggression toward people, dangerous behavior, or rule violations.
- **How:** State the boundary (calmly, in one sentence), validate the feeling, and stay close.
- **Example:** "I can't let you hit people. You're really angry. I'm staying close."
- **Critical:** The boundary is on the BEHAVIOR, not the EMOTION. Emotions are always okay. Actions have limits.

### Complexity Breaking
- **What it is:** Reducing overwhelming tasks into tiny, manageable pieces.
- **When:** During frustration or task-related meltdowns.
- **How:** "Just do [one small step] first." Pause. "Now this next step."
- **Example:** "Just put on one shoe. Don't worry about the other one yet."

### Communication Bridging
- **What it is:** Helping them find words when words are hard.
- **When:** After regulation, when they want to talk but can't express it.
- **How:** Offer options: "Are you feeling [X] or [Y]?" / "It sounds like you're saying [Z]."
- **Example:** "I'm hearing that you felt left out. Is that right?"

### Future Orientation
- **What it is:** Helping them see past the current difficulty.
- **When:** AFTER they are calm, never during dysregulation.
- **How:** "This hard feeling won't last forever." / "Tomorrow will feel different."
- **Example:** "You're really sad about this right now. Those big feelings will get smaller."

## 4.4 Tool Selection Guide
*Which tools for which states?*

---

**For State 1: Distress Seeking**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence, Physical Contact, and Validation.
- **Add:** Intentional Connection, Active Listening, Gentle Narration.
- **Skip:** Boundaries (unless behavior is dangerous), Choice Offering (too overwhelming during high distress).

**For State 2: Aggression at Objects**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence, Emotion Acknowledgment, and Validation.
- **Add:** Boundary Holding (if needed for safety), Modeling Calm, Patient Waiting.
- **After they are calm:** Gentle Reminders, Partnership Language.

**For State 3: Aggression at People**
- **Always use:** Boundary Holding and Calm Presence (after the aggression stops).
- **During aggression:** Create brief space, stay visible, and YOU stay calm.
- **After aggression stops (2-5 min):** Return close, offer Physical Contact (if accepted), and use Validation.
- **After they are fully calm (15+ min):** Use Partnership Language and Communication Bridging.

**For State 4: Shutdown**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence and Patient Waiting.
- **Add:** Gentle Narration (very brief), Modeling Calm.
- **When they are emerging:** Use Choice Offering and Intentional Connection (soft approach).
- **Skip:** Active Listening (too much of a demand), Process Praise (not the time).

## 4.5 Key Reminders
- **You can't use all 18 tools at once**, so start with 2-3 from the "Core" section.
- **Different children need different tools**, so what works for one may not work for another.
- **The same child may need different tools on different days**, as being tired, hungry, or sick changes everything.
- **Your calm is the tool underneath all the other tools**; if you're escalated, none of these will work.

# 5. What to Actually Say When It's Happening

You know the states. You have the tools. Now here's how to use them in the situations you're actually facing.

Each scenario includes what it looks like, why it's hard developmentally, and exactly what to do step-by-step with scripts you can use.

---

## 5.1 Scenario: Bedtime Resistance Meltdown
**Ages: 3-7 | State: Distress Seeking (High Intensity)**

**What it looks like:**
It's 8 p.m. You say, "Time for bed." Your child starts crying, clinging to you, begging for "5 more minutes," and escalating to screaming, unable to calm down.

**Why it's hard:**
Transitions are neurologically difficult for young children. Going from stimulating (play) to non-stimulating (sleep) requires executive function they don't have yet. Add separation anxiety (you leave the room), and bedtime becomes a double threat.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Regulate yourself (10 seconds)
- Remind yourself: *This is biology, not manipulation.*
- Take 2 slow breaths.
- Soften your voice before you speak.

### Step 2: Get close and validate (1-2 minutes)
- Kneel to eye level.
- Use physical contact (a hug if they accept it).
- **Script:** "You're really upset about bedtime. You want to keep playing. I know."

### Step 3: Set a gentle boundary (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "It's time for sleep. Your body needs rest. I'm here to help you."

### Step 4: Offer a choice within the boundary (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Do you want to bring your stuffed bear or your blankie to bed?"
- "Should we read one story or two?"

### Step 5: Co-regulate through the transition (5-10 minutes)
- Stay with them through the process.
- **Script:** "We're going to walk to your room together. I'm staying close."
- Use slow movements, a calm voice, and physical touch if they want it.

### Step 6: Use gentle narration (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Now we're putting on pajamas. Then we'll brush our teeth. Then it's story time in bed."
- Predictability reduces fear.

**Tomorrow:** Warn them earlier about the bedtime approach. 15 minutes before, say, "Bedtime is coming soon." 5 minutes before, say, "Almost bedtime, time to finish what you're doing."

## 5.2 Scenario: Sibling Toy Conflict → Hitting
**Ages: 3-6 | State: Aggression At People**

**What it looks like:**
Two children are playing. Suddenly, there is screaming. Your older child (5) just hit the younger one (3) who grabbed their toy.

**Why it's hard:**
Sharing requires impulse control, perspective-taking ("they want it too"), and delayed gratification ("I can have it later"). Children ages 3-6 don't have these skills reliably yet. Hitting is a form of communication that says, "This is mine and I don't have words for this feeling."

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Ensure safety (5 seconds)
- Move between the children physically.
- Say calmly and firmly, "No hitting. I'm keeping you both safe."

### Step 2: Create brief space (30 seconds)
- To the older child: "Sit here on this cushion. I'll be back in 30 seconds."
- To the younger child, offer quick comfort: "You got hit. That hurt. You're okay."

### Step 3: Return to the aggressor QUICKLY (within 1-2 minutes)
- Kneel to their eye level.
- Use a calm presence (you're not angry at them).
- **Script:** "You hit your sister. You were SO mad she took your toy."

### Step 4: Validate the feeling and set a boundary (1 minute)
- **Script:** "It's okay to feel angry when someone takes your thing. It's not okay to hit people."
- "Your body felt like it needed to hit. That feeling is real."

### Step 5: Co-regulate (2-5 minutes)
- Stay close, use a soft voice.
- Wait for their breathing to slow.
- Offer physical comfort if they'll accept it.

### Step 6: Problem-solve AFTER they are calm (5+ minutes later)
- **Script:** "When you feel that mad again, what could you do instead?"
- Offer options: "You could say 'I'm playing with that' or come get me for help."
- NOT: "Say sorry right now" (forced apologies don't teach).

### Step 7: Apply a natural consequence (if appropriate)
- **Script:** "Your sister felt scared when you hit her. She doesn't want to play near you right now. That's what happens when we hurt people."

**Prevention for next time:**
Teach them BEFORE a conflict: "When you don't want to share, you can say 'I'm still using this' or come get me."

## 5.3 Scenario: Public Meltdown at the Grocery Store
**Ages: 3-5 | State: Distress Seeking OR Aggression at Objects**

**What it looks like:**
You said "no" to candy. Your child collapses on the floor, screaming and kicking the cart, while people are staring.

**Why it's hard:**
Grocery stores create sensory overload (lights, sounds, people, stimulation everywhere). The prefrontal cortex is already taxed. A "no" to something they want leads to overwhelm. A public setting increases parental stress, making YOUR regulation harder.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Regulate YOURSELF first (10 seconds)
- **Internal script:** *People's opinions don't matter. My child matters. They need me.*
- Take a deep breath, drop your shoulders, and ignore the stares.

### Step 2: Get to their level (immediate)
- Kneel on the grocery store floor (yes, really).
- Use a calm, quiet voice (don't yell over their screams).

### Step 3: Validate and stay present (1-3 minutes)
- **Script:** "You really wanted that candy. You're so disappointed."
- Offer a soft touch on their back or shoulder if they'll allow it.
- DO NOT: Try to reason, threaten consequences, or drag them away.

### Step 4: Ensure safety if needed
- If they are kicking the cart or nearby people: "I'm moving the cart so you don't get hurt."
- Stay kneeling, stay calm.

### Step 5: Wait
- The intensity will peak and then decline (usually takes 3-8 minutes for young kids).
- Your calm presence speeds up this process.

### Step 6: When the crying slows (you'll see it)
- **Script:** "You're calming down. Big breaths are helping."
- "I know that was really hard."

### Step 7: Make a gentle transition (2 minutes later)
- **Script:** "We're going to finish shopping now. Do you want to sit in the cart or walk next to me?"
- If they're very young or still shaky, pick them up, carry them, and finish quickly.

**What NOT to do:**
- Give in to avoid a meltdown (this teaches them that meltdowns get them what they want).
- Threaten: "If you don't stop, we're leaving!" (empty threats undermine you).
- Isolate: Taking them to the car alone without co-regulation teaches them, "I'm alone with my feelings."

**For next time:**
Prepare them before entering the store: "We're not buying treats today. I know that's disappointing. If you feel upset, you can tell me, and I'll help you feel better."

## 5.4 Scenario: Morning Transition Chaos
**Ages: 4-7 | State: Aggression at Objects → Distress Seeking**

**What it looks like:**
It's 7:45 a.m. You need to leave for school in 15 minutes. Your child refuses to get dressed, throws their shoes, then starts crying that nothing fits right.

**Why it's hard:**
Mornings often mean low blood sugar, a tired brain, and demands on executive function (sequencing tasks, making decisions, transitioning). Clothing sensory issues are common at this age. Time pressure from a parent makes the child feel your stress, which increases their own.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Pause and regulate yourself (10 seconds)
- **Internal:** *We might be late. That's not a crisis. They need co-regulation more than punctuality right now.*
- Breathe and slow down internally.

### Step 2: Get to their level (immediate)
- Sit on the floor where they are.
- **Script:** "You're really frustrated. Getting dressed is hard this morning."

### Step 3: Validate and set a boundary on throwing (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "It's okay to feel frustrated. I can't let you throw shoes. I'm putting them over here."

### Step 4: Simplify the task (1 minute)
- **Script:** "Let's just do one thing. Can you put on your underwear? Just that. Nothing else yet."
- Break the overwhelming task into a tiny piece.

### Step 5: Offer a choice (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Do you want the blue shirt or the red one?"
- This restores a sense of control.

### Step 6: Co-regulate through completion (5 minutes)
- Stay close, slow your pace, and acknowledge each small success.
- **Script:** "Underwear on, good. Now pants. Just pants."

### Step 7: If they are still overwhelmed
- **Script:** "I'm going to help you. I'll hold the shirt, and you can put your arms through."
- Use partnership language.

**Prevention for tomorrow:**
- Lay out clothes the night before to reduce morning decisions.
- Wake up 10 minutes earlier to remove time pressure.
- Offer breakfast BEFORE dressing if low blood sugar is a pattern.

## 5.5 Scenario: Friend Conflict Aftermath, "They Don't Like Me"
**Ages: 5-7 | State: Distress Seeking (Moderate)**

**What it looks like:**
Your child comes home from school crying. Their friend played with someone else at recess. They're saying, "Nobody likes me," and, "I have no friends."

**Why it's hard:**
Social awareness is increasing at 5-7 years, but perspective-taking is still developing. They can't yet understand, "My friend can like me AND play with someone else." Their brain interprets a friend playing with another kid as rejection, which means "I'm alone forever." All-or-nothing thinking is developmentally normal.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Validate immediately (1 minute)
- Sit down and give your full attention.
- **Script:** "You're really sad. That hurt your feelings when she played with someone else."
- NOT: "You have lots of friends!" (this dismisses their current feeling).

### Step 2: Let them talk (5-10 minutes)
- Use active listening: nod, say "mm-hmm," and reflect back what they say.
- **Script:** "So you felt left out. That makes sense."

### Step 3: Acknowledge without fixing (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Feeling left out is one of the hardest feelings. I've felt that way too."
- Share a brief personal example if it's relevant: "When I was little, my friend once..."

### Step 4: AFTER they feel heard, offer gentle perspective (only when they are calm)
- **Script:** "I'm wondering, does she usually play with you, and was today different?"
- "Do you think she might still like you even though she played with someone else today?"
- Ask questions, don't make statements, to help them think.

### Step 5: Problem-solve if they want to (5 minutes later)
- **Script:** "What could you do tomorrow if this happens again?"
- "Would it help to ask, 'Can I play too?' or find a different friend to play with?"

### Step 6: Use future orientation (briefly)
- **Script:** "Friendships have hard days sometimes. Tomorrow might feel different."

**What NOT to do:**
- Minimize: "You're overreacting."
- Fix immediately: Calling the other parent to arrange a playdate.
- Reason too early: "Well, technically she CAN play with other kids" (this is true, but dismisses their feeling).

## 5.6 Scenario: Refusing to Leave the Playground
**Ages: 3-6 | State: Distress Seeking → Can escalate to Aggression**

**What it looks like:**
Park time is over. You say, "Time to go." Your child refuses, starts crying, and might throw themselves on the ground or hit you when you try to pick them up.

**Why it's hard:**
Stopping fun feels like a loss. Young children can't hold two ideas at once, like, "This is ending AND I'll come back another time." Their brain reads "fun is over forever" as a grief response.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Warn in advance (5 minutes before leaving)
- **Script:** "We're leaving in 5 minutes. You have time for a few more slides."
- Set a timer if that's helpful (a visual countdown).

### Step 2: When it's time, validate preemptively (at the transition moment)
- **Script:** "I know you don't want to leave. Leaving is hard when you're having fun."

### Step 3: If they refuse or melt down
- Kneel down, maintain a calm presence.
- **Script:** "You're really sad we're leaving. You wish we could stay longer."
- DO NOT: Argue about why you have to leave or threaten consequences.

### Step 4: If they hit you during this
- Set a boundary: "I can't let you hit me" (block hits calmly).
- **Script:** "You're SO mad. Your body feels big. It's still not okay to hit."
- Create brief space if needed (step back 2 feet, stay visible and calm).

### Step 5: Offer a choice (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "We are leaving. You can walk to the car or I can carry you. Which feels better?"
- Both options lead to the same outcome (leaving), but this restores a sense of control.

### Step 6: Follow through calmly
- If they won't choose, say, "I'm going to carry you" (pick them up calmly and gently).
- **Script while carrying:** "I know you're upset. We'll come back another day."

### Step 7: Co-regulate in the car (5 minutes)
- Let them cry if they need to.
- **Script:** "You can be sad about leaving. That feeling is okay."
- Don't lecture. Just be present.

**Prevention:**
Always give a 5-minute warning. Build in a "goodbye ritual" (wave to the playground, say "See you next time!"). This makes endings predictable.

## 5.7 Scenario: Homework Frustration, "I Can't Do It!"
**Ages: 6-7 | State: Distress Seeking → Can become Aggression at Objects**

**What it looks like:**
It's homework time. Your child tries a math problem, gets it wrong, and says, "I can't do this!" They start crying and might crumple the paper or throw their pencil.

**Why it's hard:**
This involves a cognitive load, performance pressure, and fear of failure. Children ages 6-7 are developing academic skills but also have an acute awareness of "right vs. wrong answers." The brain interprets a mistake as a threat. Frustration tolerance is still developing.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Pause the task immediately (5 seconds)
- **Script:** "Let's stop for a minute. This is feeling too hard right now."

### Step 2: Validate and normalize (30 seconds)
- Get to their eye level.
- **Script:** "You're really frustrated. This problem is tricky. That makes sense."
- "Everyone gets stuck on hard things. Even me."

### Step 3: Co-regulate (2-5 minutes)
- Sit next to them (not across from them, to be on the same team).
- Use a calm presence and slow breathing.
- **Script:** "Let's both take some slow breaths. We're not in a hurry."

### Step 4: Break down the complexity (when they are calmer)
- **Script:** "Let's do this one tiny step at a time. What's the first number in this problem?"
- "Just that. Don't worry about the rest yet."

### Step 5: Use partnership language
- **Script:** "We can figure this out together. I'm here if you need help."
- NOT: "You should know this" or "Try harder."

### Step 6: Use process praise (when they make progress)
- **Script:** "You kept trying even when it was frustrating. That took real effort."

**Prevention:**
Build in breaks every 10-15 minutes. Offer a snack before homework, as low blood sugar can lead to low frustration tolerance. Notice their capacity that day; if they're exhausted, shorten the homework time.

## 5.8 Scenario: Screen Time Ending Meltdown
**Ages: 3-7 | State: Distress Seeking OR Aggression at Objects**

**What it looks like:**
Screen time is over. You turn off the tablet or TV. Your child screams, might throw the device, hits the couch, or sobs inconsolably.

**Why it's hard:**
Screens provide high dopamine stimulation. Turning them off can feel like withdrawal, as the brain wants that stimulation back. The transition from high stimulation to low is neurologically jarring and feels like a loss.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Warn before it ends (5- and 2-minute warnings)
- 5 minutes before: "Screen time ends in 5 minutes. Almost time to stop."
- 2 minutes before: "2 more minutes, then all done."
- Set a visible timer if helpful.

### Step 2: When you turn it off (the transition moment)
- Use a calm, matter-of-fact tone.
- **Script:** "Screen time is over now. I'm turning it off."
- DO NOT: Apologize, negotiate, or explain excessively.

### Step 3: Expect upset and validate (immediate)
- **Script:** "You're really mad I turned it off. You wanted to keep watching."
- Get close (kneel down).

### Step 4: If there is aggression at objects (throwing device/hitting furniture)
- Set a boundary: "I can't let you throw the tablet. I'm putting it up high."
- **Script:** "You're SO frustrated. Your body feels big and angry."

### Step 5: Offer a physical discharge option (1 minute)
- **Script:** "Your body has a lot of energy. Do you want to jump on the trampoline or run around outside?"
- Redirect the intensity to a safe outlet.

### Step 6: Co-regulate through the feeling (3-10 minutes)
- Stay close, let them be upset.
- **Script:** "I'm here while you have this big feeling. It's okay to be mad."
- Be patient; the feeling WILL pass.

### Step 7: When they are calm (10+ minutes later)
- **Script:** "That was really hard for you. Tomorrow, screen time will end again, and I'll help you with the feeling."

**Prevention:**
Use consistent limits (the same amount of screen time daily makes it predictable). Use a timer so YOU'RE not the "bad guy" (the timer says stop, not the parent). Offer an engaging alternative immediately after, like going outside or playing a game together.

## 5.9 Scenario: "It's Not Fair!" Protest
**Ages: 5-7 | State: Distress Seeking (Moderate)**

**What it looks like:**
A sibling got something they didn't, or a rule applies to them but not to an older sibling. Your child is crying, yelling "It's not fair!" and won't let it go.

**Why it's hard:**
Children ages 5-7 develop an ACUTE awareness of fairness but can't yet understand that different ages mean different needs or rules. Their brain sees "they got X, I didn't" as an injustice, a threat to their place in the family. They use concrete thinking and can't grasp nuance.

**What to do:**

### Step 1: Validate the feeling (immediate)
- **Script:** "You're really upset. It feels unfair that she got to [X] and you didn't."
- NOT: "Life's not fair, get used to it" (this is true but dismissive).

### Step 2: Acknowledge their logic (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "You're thinking that since she got it, you should too. That makes sense to you."

### Step 3: Give a brief explanation (only if they're calm enough)
- **Script:** "She's 8 and you're 5. Sometimes different ages mean different rules."
- "When you're 8, this rule will be different for you too."
- Keep it short; long lectures don't work.

### Step 4: If they're still upset (1-2 minutes)
- **Script:** "I hear that you're still angry about this. That feeling is real."
- Use active listening and stay close.

### Step 5: Use future orientation (when they are calmer)
- **Script:** "This feels really unfair right now. That feeling won't last forever."

### Step 6: Redirect if they are still stuck
- **Script:** "I know you're upset about this. Do you want to [do an alternative activity] or stay close to me for a bit?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Over-explain or justify repeatedly (this creates a power struggle).
- Change the rule to appease them (this teaches them that protesting gets them what they want).
- Dismiss them: "You're being ridiculous."

Every scenario follows the same structure:
1.  Regulate yourself first.

# 6. Common Problems and Solutions

You're trying co-regulation. Sometimes it works, and sometimes it doesn't. Here's why and what to do about it.

---

## 6.1 Problem: "I Can't Stay Calm When They're Screaming"

**Why this is hard:**
Your own nervous system responds to their dysregulation. Your amygdala activates (threat detection). Biologically, you're wired to REACT to a screaming child.

**What's actually happening:**
You're getting dysregulated BY their dysregulation. You can't regulate them if you're escalated.

**Solutions:**

**Immediate (in the moment):**
- Take a 60-second break if it's safe (go to the bathroom, outside, or a different room).
- Hand off to a partner if available: "I need a minute. Can you cover?"
- Do a physical reset: splash cold water on your face, shake out your body.
- **Internal script:** *Their dysregulation is not an emergency. I can handle this. Slow down.*

**Long-term:**
- Notice YOUR triggers. What situations make you lose it? (Public meltdowns? Hitting? Defiance?)
- Build your own regulation capacity through therapy, exercise, sleep, or a support system.
- Practice during low-stakes moments (when they whine about dinner, practice staying calm).
- **Reality:** You won't be calm every time. Repair when you mess up (see the "repair script" below).

---

## 6.2 Problem: "They Escalate More When I Stay Calm"

**Why this happens:**
They're testing whether you'll stay steady. If your previous pattern was that you yell and then they stop (out of fear), your NEW calm confuses them. They push harder to see if you'll crack.

**What's actually happening:**
This is an **extinction burst**. When a behavior that used to work suddenly doesn't, the behavior temporarily gets WORSE before it gets better. This is normal and expected. It means it's working.

**Solutions:**

**What to do:**
- **Keep going**; do NOT return to yelling.
- Stay calm even when the intensity increases.
- **Internal script:** *This is the extinction burst. This is temporary. Consistency builds trust.*
- **Script to child:** "I'm staying calm even though you're very upset. I'm not going anywhere."

**Timeline:**
Expect 1-2 weeks of "testing." If you stay consistent, the intensity will decrease. Inconsistency (being calm sometimes but yelling other times) makes it worse and last longer.

---

## 6.3 Problem: "This Takes Forever, Punishment Was Faster"

**Why you're feeling this:**
Punishment stops behavior through fear, which gets fast results. Co-regulation builds capacity through repetition, which gets slow results.

**What's actually happening:**
You're comparing different goals:
- The goal of punishment is to stop this behavior right now (short-term compliance).
- The goal of co-regulation is to build emotional regulation capacity (a long-term skill).

**The truth:**
Punishment IS faster in the moment. But it doesn't teach regulation. It teaches children to hide their emotions, fear the adult, and comply to avoid consequences.

**Solutions:**

**Mindset shift:**
- You're not managing behavior; you're building their nervous system.
- Fast isn't always better. Effective is better.
- **Timeline:** It takes 2-4 weeks of co-regulation to see consistent changes. Stick with it.

**Realistic expectations:**
- **First week:** It will feel impossible, and you'll want to quit.
- **Weeks 2-3:** You'll see small changes (meltdowns are slightly shorter, they recover faster).
- **Week 4+:** You'll notice a difference (they start asking for help, can sometimes self-soothe).

---

## 6.4 Problem: "My Partner/Parents Undermine This Approach"

**Why this happens:**
Your family members see co-regulation as "too soft." They say things like, "You're spoiling them," "They need real consequences," or "We turned out fine after being spanked."

**What's actually happening:**
They're defending the methods that were used on them. Examining parenting means examining their own childhood trauma, which can be threatening.

**Solutions:**

**What you CAN control:**
- YOUR response to your child.
- Modeling co-regulation consistently.
- **Script to family:** "I'm trying a different approach based on child development research. I need you to support that, even if you don't agree."

**What you CAN'T control:**
- Their parenting opinions.
- Whether they change their methods.
- What they do when they're alone with your child.

**The reality:**
- Children adapt to different adult styles.
- Your child can learn, "Mom does it this way, and Grandma does it differently."
- **Consistency from YOU matters more than everyone agreeing.**
- Remember: it is YOUR child, your decision, and your responsibility. Whatever anyone else says is just their opinion. You have the final say.

**If your partner is the issue:**
- Share Section 2 of this guide (the brain science legitimizes the approach).
- Try co-regulation together for 2 weeks as an experiment.
- If conflict continues, consider couples therapy or a parenting coach.

---

## 6.5 Problem: "My Child Has ADHD/Autism/Sensory Issues, Does This Still Work?"

**Short answer: Yes. It is even more important for neurodivergent nervous systems.**

**Why:**
Neurodivergent children often have:
- More intense emotional responses (bigger feelings).
- Longer dysregulation times (it takes longer to calm down).
- Different sensory needs (some want deep pressure, some want zero touch).
- Executive function challenges (it is harder to sequence "calm down" steps alone).

**Adaptations:**

**For ADHD:**
- Expect longer regulation times (15-30 minutes is common).
- Physical movement helps (let them pace, jump, or fidget WHILE you stay calm nearby).
- Keep words minimal (their processing is slower when they are dysregulated).

**For Autism/SPD:**
- Learn THEIR sensory regulation needs (deep pressure? a weighted blanket? dim lights? silence?).
- Shutdown is more common; respect their need for space while staying nearby.
- Visual supports can help (an emotion chart, a timer, pictures of coping strategies).

**For all neurodivergent kids:**
- Co-regulation is THE tool, as they need external regulation support longer and more intensely.
- Adjust the tools to their nervous system (some need more touch, some need less).
- Validation is critical, as they often get the message that their reactions are wrong.

---

## 6.6 Problem: "I Messed Up and Yelled/Lost My Temper. Now What?"

**You will mess up. We all do, and that's okay. You don't need to be perfect for it to work. The important part is what you do after.**

**What to do (Repair Protocol):**

### Step 1: Regulate yourself first (5-10 minutes)
- Walk away and calm down. Don't try to repair while you're still escalated.

### Step 2: Return and name what you did (1 minute)
- Get to their eye level.
- **Script:** "I yelled at you earlier. That wasn't okay. I was feeling overwhelmed and I lost my calm."

### Step 3: Take responsibility (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "That was my mistake. You didn't make me yell. I chose to yell because I didn't use my tools."
- NOT: "You made me so mad that I..." (this is blaming).

### Step 4: Acknowledge the impact (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "When I yell, it probably feels scary. I'm sorry I did that."

### Step 5: Make a future plan (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Next time I feel that frustrated, I'm going to [walk away for 60 seconds / take deep breaths] instead of yelling."

### Step 6: Reconnect (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Are you okay? Do you want a hug?"
- Offer physical reconnection if they want it.

**Why repair matters:**
- It models how adults make mistakes, take responsibility, and repair relationships.
- It teaches that mistakes don't end a connection and models how to correct them.
- **Repairs are MORE powerful teaching moments than "getting it right."**

---

## 6.7 When to Get Professional Help

**Seek support from a child psychologist, therapist, or parenting coach if:**

❗ **Your child's aggression:**
- Injures others repeatedly despite consistent co-regulation.
- Targets the same person (you, a sibling) with intense aggression daily.
- Includes self-harm (hitting self, head-banging).

❗ **The duration of dysregulation:**
- Lasts 2+ hours regularly.
- Happens 5+ times daily for weeks.
- Shows no improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent co-regulation.

❗ **Your dysregulation:**
- You cannot stay calm despite trying.
- You're hitting, yelling, or using physical force regularly.
- You feel rage, hatred, or the wish to harm your child.
- You're so overwhelmed you can't function.

❗ **Relationship breakdown:**
- You feel no connection to your child.
- Your child seems afraid of you.
- The family system is collapsing under stress.

❗ **Safety concerns:**
- The child hurts siblings badly enough to need medical care.

# 7. You Have the Information. Now What?

Reading this guide won't change anything. **Using it will.**

Here's how to move from knowing to doing.

---

## 7.1 This Week: Start Small

**Pick ONE scenario you face regularly:**
- Bedtime battles?
- Morning chaos?
- Sibling fights?
- Public meltdowns?

**Pick TWO tools from Section 4:**
- Calm Presence (always)
- Plus Validation, Physical Contact, OR Boundary Holding (depending on your scenario)

**Use the 3 R's protocol (from Section 1) for that ONE situation this week.**

That's it. Don't try to fix everything at once.

---

## 7.2 Track What Changes

Answer these questions after 1 week:

**Dysregulation duration:**
- How long do meltdowns last now vs. before you started?
- Are they getting shorter? (Even 2 minutes shorter is progress).

**Your regulation:**
- Are YOU staying calmer?
- Even one less yelling episode this week is a success.

**Child's awareness:**
- Can your child name their emotions yet? ("I'm angry" / "I feel sad").
- Are they starting to ask for help? ("I need a hug" / "I'm feeling big").

**Don't expect:**
- Zero meltdowns.
- Perfect compliance.
- Immediate transformation.

**Do expect:**
- Slightly shorter dysregulation times.
- Moments where it works beautifully.
- Moments where it fails completely (that's normal, so keep going).

---

## 7.3 Weeks 2-4: Build Gradually

**Add 1-2 more tools** from Section 4.

**Apply the tools to a second scenario** you face regularly.

**Notice patterns:**
- What time of day is hardest? (Being tired means more dysregulation).
- What triggers are predictable? (Transitions, hunger, overstimulation).
- Which tools work best for YOUR child?

**Adjust:**
- If some tools don't work for your kid, skip them.
- If some tools work, use them more.
- Every child is different, so adapt.

---

## 7.4 This Is Hard. You're Doing It Anyway.

**On hard days, remember:**

❌ You're not aiming for perfect.
✅ You're aiming for better than yesterday.

❌ You won't use these tools flawlessly every time.
✅ You'll use them more often than you did before.

❌ Your child won't be "fixed."
✅ Your child will build capacity over years.

**You're building their nervous system, one co-regulation moment at a time.**

**Hard is not impossible.**

## **When Your Child Is Dysregulated RIGHT NOW**

Your child is screaming, hitting, throwing things, or melting down. It's called dysregulation. You need to know what to do in the next 60 seconds.

### The 3 R's: Regulate, Relate, Reason


---

### **STEP 1: REGULATE YOURSELF FIRST** 
**Time: 30 seconds**

Before you do anything else, calm your own nervous system. A child cannot self-regulate (calm down on their own, control their emotions). They do it through you being calm. A dysregulated adult cannot regulate a dysregulated child.

**Do this:**
- Take 3 slow breaths (in through nose, out through mouth)
- Drop your shoulders
- Lower your voice tone (even if you haven't spoken yet)
- Think: *"Their brain can't handle this alone yet. They need me calm to calm down. This is biology, not behavior."*

**Why this matters:** Your child's nervous system reads YOUR nervous system. If you're escalated, they stay escalated. Your calm is the first step.

---

### **STEP 2: REGULATE + RELATE TOGETHER**
**Time: 1-10 minutes (sometimes longer—normal)**

Approach your child. Provide calm presence AND validate their emotion at the same time. These happen together, not in sequence.

**Physical presence (Regulate):**
- Get to their eye level (kneel, sit)
- Move close (within arm's reach)
- Offer touch if they accept it (hug, hand on back, holding hand)
- If they pull away, stay nearby without touching, don't force contact
- Use soft, slow voice tone

**Words (Relate/Validate):**
- **Name the feeling:** "You're so angry." / "That's really scary." / "You're frustrated."
- **Make it okay:** "It's okay to feel that." / "Big feelings are hard."
- **Stay simple:** "I'm here." / "You're safe." / "It it going to be fine soon."

**Examples of Regulate + Relate together:**
- Child runs to you crying → You kneel and hug them → "You're really scared. It's okay. I've got you."
- Child throwing toys → You sit nearby → "You're so frustrated right now. That's really hard."

<!-- This needs improvement -->
- Child hits you → You create space → "You're very angry. I won't let you hit me, and I'm staying close."

**DO NOT (yet):**
- Ask "why" questions
- Lecture or explain rules
- Give consequences
- Problem-solve
- Demand apologies or eye contact

**Why:** Their prefrontal cortex (logic brain) is offline. They cannot process reasoning. They need your calm + validation to help their nervous system return to baseline.

---

### **STEP 3: REASON (When They're Calmer)**
**Time: 5+ minutes after meltdown starts (sometimes 30+ minutes)**

**Wait for these signs they're ready:**
- Breathing has slowed
- Crying is less intense or stopped
- They can make eye contact
- They respond to simple questions

**NOW you can reason:**
- Ask what happened: "Can you tell me what was hard?"
- Problem-solve together: "What could help next time?"
- Offer choices: "Do you need more hug time or some space?"
- Set boundaries if needed: "You can feel angry. You cannot hit people."
- Repair if needed: "I yelled earlier. That wasn't okay. I'm sorry."

**Why:** Their higher brain (logic, problem-solving) is back online. Now they can learn.

---

- **Regulate + Relate happen together** (calm presence + emotion validation)
- **Reason happens after** (when they're calm enough to think)
- Regulation time varies wildly: 2 minutes to 2 hours (both normal)
- Younger = longer regulation time
- Your presence is the tool, not your words

# **SECTION 2: Why This Works - The Science (Pages 5-7)**

---

## **Your Child's Brain Can't Self-Regulate Yet (And That's Normal)**

You're not failing. Your child isn't broken. Their brain is still building the parts that manage big emotions.

Understanding why co-regulation works will help you stay consistent when it's hard.

---

### **2.1 Brain Development Timeline**

**The part of the brain that handles emotional regulation doesn't finish developing until the mid-20s.**

Here's what's happening in your 3-7 year old:

**The Amygdala (Emotion Center):**
- Fully online and highly active
- Detects threats (real or perceived)
- Triggers big emotional responses
- Develops FASTER than the regulation centers

**The Prefrontal Cortex (Logic & Self-Control Center):**
- Still under construction (won't mature until mid-20s)
- Responsible for: planning, impulse control, emotional regulation, logical thinking
- In ages 3-7: Building connections, NOT fully functional yet
- Goes completely OFFLINE during stress

**What this means:**
Your child has a fully operational emotion-generator (amygdala) but an underdeveloped emotion-manager (prefrontal cortex).

**Result:** Big emotions with no built-in management system.

This is why they melt down over "small" things. To their brain, it's not small—it's overwhelming, and they have no internal capacity to handle it alone.

---

- **Amygdala (highlighted, active):** "Emotion Center - FULLY ONLINE"
- **Prefrontal Cortex (grayed out, under construction):** "Self-Control Center - STILL BUILDING"
- **Arrow showing:** "Won't mature until mid-20s"]

---

### **2.2 What Co-Regulation Actually Is**

**Co-regulation: Your calm nervous system helps regulate their developing one.**

This isn't a parenting style. It's biology.

**How it works:**
- Your child's nervous system is immature
- During dysregulation, their stress response (fight/flight/freeze) activates
- They cannot calm themselves—their brain literally doesn't have the wiring yet
- Your calm, regulated nervous system provides the external regulation they need
- Over thousands of repetitions, they internalize this capacity
- Eventually, they develop self-regulation (around ages 7-12, continuing into adulthood)

**The developmental path:**
- **Ages 0-3:** Full co-regulation (you do all the regulating)
- **Ages 3-5:** Heavy scaffolding (you're there, they're starting to practice)
- **Ages 5-7:** Coaching (they can sometimes regulate with your support)
- **Ages 7+:** Guided independence (they're building self-regulation, still need backup)

**Research evidence:**
Attachment science shows that **responsive caregiving** (staying calm, validating emotions, providing comfort) leads to:
- Secure attachment
- Better emotional regulation in childhood
- Healthier stress responses in adulthood
- Ability to form healthy relationships

Children who receive co-regulation learn: *"Emotions are manageable. I can handle this. Adults will help me."*

Children who DON'T receive co-regulation learn: *"Emotions are emergencies. I'm alone in this. I must hide what I feel."*

---

### **2.3 Why Punishment Doesn't Work**

When you punish a dysregulated child, here's what happens in their brain:

**1. Amygdala activates HARDER** (punishment = threat)
- Fear response intensifies
- Stress hormones flood the system
- Fight/flight/freeze deepens

**2. Prefrontal cortex shuts down COMPLETELY**
- Cannot access logic
- Cannot learn the lesson you're trying to teach
- Cannot connect behavior to consequence rationally

**3. They learn the wrong lesson:**
- ❌ NOT: "I shouldn't hit when I'm angry"
- ✅ INSTEAD: "My emotions are dangerous and I must hide them"
- ✅ ALSO: "Coming to an adult makes it worse, I can't trust them"

**What punishment actually teaches:**
- Compliance through fear (not self-regulation)
- Emotional suppression (not emotional management)
- Adult authority is based on power (not on earned respect)
- Adults are a source of danger to avoid, not a source of help to approach

**The long-term result:**
Adults who can't regulate their internal reality. They never learned how—they learned to hide it.

---

**Co-regulation teaches actual regulation:**
- ✅ "My emotions are signals, not emergencies"
- ✅ "I can handle big feelings with support"
- ✅ "Adults help me when I'm overwhelmed. I can trust them. I can open up."
- ✅ Eventually: "I can manage this myself"

---

### **What You're Building Over Time**

Co-regulation is temporary. You're teaching them to do this themselves.

**The progression:**

**Ages 3-4:**
- You provide full regulation support
- They're learning: emotions have names, feelings pass, adults stay calm

**Ages 5-6:**
- You're scaffolding (present but they're practicing)
- They're learning: "I'm angry" identification, simple coping tools, asking for help

**Ages 6-7:**
- You're coaching (they can do it, you're backup)
- They're learning: self-soothing strategies, problem-solving, emotional recovery

**Ages 7+:**
- They're building true self-regulation
- You're still there when it's too big, but they can handle more alone

---

**This process takes YEARS. That's not a flaw—it's development.**

Your job isn't to make them self-regulate at age 4. Your job is to co-regulate so consistently that by age 10, they've internalized the capacity.

- You're not creating dependency—you're building capacity
- The time you invest now = emotional health for life

# **SECTION 3: Reading Your Child - The 4 Dysregulation States (Pages 8-11)**

---

## **What You're Actually Seeing + What To Do**

Dysregulation doesn't look the same every time. Your child's emotional state changes based on how overwhelmed they are and how they express it.

Your response needs to match what you're seeing. This section teaches you to read the 4 different dysregulation states and adapt your approach.

---

### **The Science:**

Research shows children express dysregulation in two main ways:

**Internalizing:** Distress directed inward (seeking comfort, withdrawing)  
**Externalizing:** Distress directed outward (aggression, physical discharge)

Within these, the intensity and target of the behavior varies.

Understanding which state you're seeing helps you choose the right response.

---

## **THE 4 DYSREGULATION STATES**

---

### **STATE 1: Distress Seeking (Internalizing)**

**What it looks like:**

**High Intensity:**
- Crying intensely, can't catch breath
- Clinging to you, won't let go
- Running TO you for help
- Terror, panic, overwhelming sadness
- Cannot talk or form words

**Moderate Intensity:**
- Upset but can talk (even if voice shakes)
- Seeking you out, approaching you
- Verbal about what's wrong ("I'm scared" / "That hurt my feelings")
- Sad, worried, frustrated—but still somewhat in control

**What's happening in their brain:**
Emotional overwhelm. Their internal distress is too big to handle alone. They're seeking your nervous system to help regulate theirs.

**What to do:**

**For High Intensity:**
1. **Get close immediately** (pick up if young enough, kneel down if not)
2. **Physical contact** (hug, hold, rock, hand on back—whatever they accept)
3. **Calm presence** (slow breathing, soft voice, steady)
4. **Validate while regulating:** "You're so scared. It's okay. I've got you." / "You're really sad. I'm here."
5. **Stay until breathing slows** (2-20 minutes, sometimes longer)

**For Moderate Intensity:**
1. **Get to eye level**
2. **Validate the emotion:** "You're really frustrated about this." / "That does feel disappointing."
3. **Stay physically close** (sit nearby, hand on shoulder)
4. **Support their coping tools:** "Do you want to try your deep breaths?" / "Would a hug help?"
5. **Listen** (let them talk it out if they want)

**Don't:** Dismiss ("It's not a big deal"), rush to fix it, or leave them alone to "calm down"

**Why this works:** They're asking for help regulating. Your calm presence + validation = their nervous system can return to baseline.

---

### **STATE 2: Aggression Directed At Objects/Environment (Externalizing - Undirected)**

**What it looks like:**
- Throwing toys, books, or objects (not AT people)
- Hitting furniture, walls, or floor
- Screaming, flailing, kicking at objects
- Physical discharge of overwhelm
- NOT seeking you out
- NOT directing aggression at people—just explosive, reactive behavior

**What's happening in their brain:**
Overwhelmed nervous system trying to discharge intensity through physical action. This is **reactive aggression**—impulsive, emotional response to frustration. Not planned, not goal-directed.

**What to do:**
1. **Ensure safety first** (move breakables, protect siblings, remove dangerous objects)
2. **Set boundary if needed** (calm, firm, one sentence): "I can't let you throw that. I'm moving it here."
3. **Get close when safe** (kneel nearby, soft voice)
4. **Validate the emotion:** "You're SO angry right now. Your body feels like it needs to move."
5. **Offer calm presence** (stay close, breathe slowly, wait)
6. **After calm: Investigate trigger** (5+ minutes later): "What happened before you got so upset?"

**Don't:** Yell, match their intensity, give consequences during the meltdown, or physically restrain unless safety requires it

**Why this works:** Physical discharge helps some children regulate. Your boundary creates safety. Your calm + validation = they learn their intensity is manageable.

---

### **STATE 3: Aggression Directed At People (Externalizing + Targeted)**

**What it looks like:**
- Hitting YOU, siblings, or other people
- Kicking, biting, scratching someone
- Yelling hurtful words AT a specific person
- Aggression is targeted at a person, not objects

**What's happening in their brain:**
Full amygdala activation. This is **reactive aggression**—impulsive, emotionally driven. They're overwhelmed and the person (you, sibling, peer) has become associated with their frustration. Not planned. Not calculated. Brain is in survival mode.

**What to do:**

**1. Protect immediately** (5-10 seconds)
- Block hits calmly (catch hand, step back)
- DO NOT hit back or yell
- For sibling aggression: Move between children physically
- Calm, firm voice: "I can't let you hurt [me/them]."

**2. Create brief space if needed for safety** (30 seconds - 2 minutes MAX)
- If aggression continues despite boundary: "I'm moving back so we're both safe."
- Stay in visual contact (across room, in doorway, nearby)
- YOU stay calm (deep breaths, drop shoulders)
- Watch for aggression to stop

**3. Return with calm presence AS SOON AS aggression stops** (within 2-5 minutes)
- Do NOT leave them alone to "figure it out"
- Reconnect quickly: "The hitting stopped. I'm coming back close now."
- Get close again (kneel, sit nearby)

**4. Regulate together** (2-10 minutes)
- Soft voice: "You were very angry."
- "It's okay to feel angry. It's not okay to hit people."
- Validate emotion, set boundary on behavior
- Offer physical comfort if they'll accept it (hug, hand on back)
- Stay until breathing slows

**5. After FULLY calm: Address what happened** (15+ minutes later)
- "What was happening that made you so mad?"
- "When you feel that angry again, what could you do instead of hitting?"
- If sibling: Both children need support (victim gets comfort first, aggressor gets co-regulation)

**Don't:**
- Leave them alone for long periods
- Yell, threaten, or hit back
- Demand immediate apologies mid-dysregulation
- Lecture while they're still escalated
- Use the space as punishment ("Go think about what you did")

**Why this works:**
- Brief space = immediate safety for both people (NOT abandonment)
- Your quick return = "I won't leave you alone in your overwhelm"
- Staying calm = you model the regulation they need to learn
- Validating feeling + clear boundary = they learn "emotions are okay, hurting people is not"

---

**[Critical Distinction Box:]**

### **Brief Safety Space vs. Punitive Time-Out**

**❌ What DOESN'T work (Punitive):**
- "Go to your room until you can behave"
- Leaving them alone for 10+ minutes
- Withdrawal of connection as punishment
- Ignoring them completely

**✅ What DOES work (Co-regulation with space):**
- "I'm creating space so neither of us gets hurt"
- Staying visible, staying calm
- Returning within 2-5 minutes (as soon as hitting stops)
- Reconnecting to provide regulation support

**The key:** Space is temporary and for safety only. You return QUICKLY to co-regulate. The child is never left alone to manage overwhelming emotion.

---

### **STATE 4: Shutdown (Internalizing + Freeze)**

**What it looks like:**
- Blank stare, unresponsive to questions
- Won't talk, won't make eye contact
- Sitting motionless, hiding, or curled up
- Not crying, not angry—just...gone
- May look "calm" but they're actually overwhelmed

**What's happening in their brain:**
Nervous system has shut down. This is the **freeze response**—when fight and flight aren't options, the brain protects itself by shutting down. Overwhelm that looks quiet but isn't.

**What to do:**
1. **Sit nearby** (close enough they know you're there, not touching unless they initiate)
2. **Soft, quiet words** (very few): "I'm here when you're ready." / "You're safe."
3. **No demands** (don't ask questions, don't require eye contact or responses)
4. **Wait** (could be 5 minutes, could be 45 minutes—stay patient)
5. **Notice signs they're emerging** (slight movement, looking around, eye contact)
6. **Offer gentle connection when ready:** "Hi. I'm here. Do you want to sit together?"

**Don't:** Force talking, interpret silence as "fine now," demand explanations, or rush them

**Why this works:** Shutdown = freeze response to unbearable overwhelm. Your non-demanding, safe presence = their nervous system can slowly come back online without added pressure.

|-----------|------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Distress Seeking** | Crying, clinging, seeking you out | Internalizing behavior | Full co-reg: presence + physical comfort + validation |

- The same child can show different states at different times

# **SECTION 4: Your Co-Regulation Toolbox (Pages 12-15)**

---

## **The Moves That Actually Work**

These are the specific actions you take during co-regulation. They're not scripts to memorize—they're tools you adapt to your child's needs.

**Start simple:** Pick 2-3 tools. Practice those until they feel natural. Then add more.

**Foundation:** Your own calm is required for ALL of these tools to work. If you're dysregulated, regulate yourself first (3 deep breaths, drop shoulders, slow your voice, remember: they are not your enemy, they just can't deal with emotions yet, they need help).

---

### **CORE REGULATION TOOLS**
*Use these during any dysregulation state*

---

**Calm Presence**
- **What it is:** Staying physically close with a regulated nervous system
- **When:** ALL states—this is the foundation
- **How:** Breathe slowly, soften your face, lower your shoulders, stay within arm's reach
- **Example:** Child is melting down → You sit on floor nearby, breathing calmly, saying nothing yet

**Physical Contact**
- **What it is:** Touch that soothes and regulates
- **When:** Distress seeking (always), aggression (after it stops), shutdown (only if they initiate)
- **How:** Hug, hand on back, holding hand, rocking together—whatever they accept
- **Example:** "Would a hug help?" / Simply opening your arms if they approach
- **Note:** Some children pull away when upset—respect that, stay close without touching

**Intentional Connection**
- **What it is:** Getting to their level with soft voice and gentle approach
- **When:** Beginning of ANY co-regulation
- **How:** Kneel or sit so you're at eye level, speak softly and slowly, move toward them gently
- **Example:** Child crying → You kneel down, soft voice: "Hey. I'm here."

**Emotion Acknowledgment**
- **What it is:** Naming what you observe without judgment
- **When:** After you're close, while regulating
- **How:** State what you see: "You look really angry." / "You seem scared."
- **Example:** Throwing toys → "You're so frustrated right now."

**Feeling Validation**
- **What it is:** Making emotions okay to have
- **When:** During Regulate + Relate phase (Steps 1-2 of the 3 R's)
- **How:** "It's okay to feel [emotion]." / "Big feelings are hard." / "That makes sense."
- **Example:** "It's okay to be mad that your tower fell down. You worked hard on that."

**Active Listening**
- **What it is:** Full attention with minimal words, reflecting back what they say
- **When:** After they're calmer and want to talk
- **How:** Nod, "mm-hmm," repeat their words back, maintain eye contact
- **Example:** Child: "She took my toy!" → You: "She took your toy. That felt unfair."

---

### **BUILDING TOOLS**
*Use these as you get comfortable with core tools*

---

**Gentle Narration**
- **What it is:** Explaining what's happening to reduce uncertainty
- **When:** During distress, during transitions, before potentially scary things
- **How:** Calm voice, simple words: "First we'll [X], then [Y]."
- **Example:** "The doctor will look in your ears. It won't hurt, just feels weird."

**Process Praise**
- **What it is:** Acknowledging effort/strategy, not outcome
- **When:** AFTER regulation, during calm moments, catching positive behavior
- **How:** Name what they DID: "You kept trying different ways." / "You used your words."
- **Example:** NOT "Good job!" → INSTEAD "You asked for help instead of hitting. That took work."

**Choice Offering**
- **What it is:** Giving small, real choices to restore sense of control
- **When:** During moderate distress, after aggression stops, during shutdown emergence
- **How:** Two options (both acceptable to you): "Do you want [X] or [Y]?"
- **Example:** "Do you want to sit on my lap or next to me?" / "Hug or space?"

**Mistake Normalizing**
- **What it is:** Framing errors as learning, not failures
- **When:** After calm, addressing what happened
- **How:** "Mistakes help us learn." / "Everyone has hard moments."
- **Example:** "You hit your sister. That's not okay, AND you were really overwhelmed. Let's figure out what to do next time."

**Patient Waiting**
- **What it is:** Giving them time to process without rushing
- **When:** Shutdown, when they need to think, when they're almost calm
- **How:** Silence. Presence. No hurry. Let them set the pace.
- **Example:** Child is staring at floor → You sit quietly nearby for 5+ minutes without speaking

**Modeling Calm**
- **What it is:** Demonstrating regulated breathing and peaceful energy
- **When:** Throughout ALL dysregulation
- **How:** Slow your breathing visibly, relax your body, speak in slow, low tones
- **Example:** "I'm going to take a slow breath. You can try with me if you want."

---

### **ADVANCED TOOLS**
*Add these when you're solid with the core tools*

---

**Gentle Reminders**
- **What it is:** Helping them remember coping strategies they know
- **When:** Moderate distress, when they're starting to calm
- **How:** Soft voice, question format: "What helped last time you felt this way?"
- **Example:** "Remember your deep breathing? Want to try that?"

**Partnership Language**
- **What it is:** Using "we" to show you're on the same team
- **When:** After calm, during problem-solving
- **How:** "We can figure this out together." / "Let's see what helps."
- **Example:** NOT "You need to stop crying" → "We'll work through this feeling together."

**Boundary Holding**
- **What it is:** Clear, kind limits while still offering emotional support
- **When:** Aggression at people, dangerous behavior, rule violations
- **How:** State boundary (calm, one sentence) + validate feeling + stay close
- **Example:** "I can't let you hit people. You're really angry. I'm staying close."
- **Critical:** Boundary on BEHAVIOR, not on EMOTION. Emotions are always okay. Actions have limits.

**Complexity Breaking**
- **What it is:** Reducing overwhelming tasks into tiny, manageable pieces
- **When:** Frustration, task-related meltdowns
- **How:** "Just do [one small step] first." Pause. "Now this next step."
- **Example:** "Just put on one shoe. Don't worry about the other one yet."

**Communication Bridging**
- **What it is:** Helping them find words when words are hard
- **When:** After regulation, when they want to talk but can't express it
- **How:** Offer options: "Are you feeling [X] or [Y]?" / "It sounds like you're saying [Z]."
- **Example:** "I'm hearing that you felt left out. Is that right?"

**Future Orientation**
- **What it is:** Helping them see past the current difficulty
- **When:** AFTER calm, never during dysregulation
- **How:** "This hard feeling won't last forever." / "Tomorrow will feel different."
- **Example:** "You're really sad about this right now. Those big feelings will get smaller."

---

### **TOOL SELECTION GUIDE**
*Which tools for which states?*

---

**For STATE 1: Distress Seeking**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence + Physical Contact + Validation
- **Add:** Intentional Connection, Active Listening, Gentle Narration
- **Skip:** Boundaries (unless behavior is dangerous), Choice Offering (too overwhelming when high distress)

**For STATE 2: Aggression At Objects**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence + Emotion Acknowledgment + Validation
- **Add:** Boundary Holding (if needed for safety), Modeling Calm, Patient Waiting
- **After calm:** Gentle Reminders, Partnership Language

**For STATE 3: Aggression At People**
- **Always use:** Boundary Holding + Calm Presence (after aggression stops)
- **During aggression:** Create brief space, stay visible, YOU stay calm
- **After aggression stops (2-5 min):** Return close, Physical Contact (if accepted), Validation
- **After fully calm (15+ min):** Partnership Language, Communication Bridging

**For STATE 4: Shutdown**
- **Always use:** Calm Presence + Patient Waiting
- **Add:** Gentle Narration (very brief), Modeling Calm
- **When emerging:** Choice Offering, Intentional Connection (soft approach)
- **Skip:** Active Listening (too much demand), Process Praise (not the time)

- **You can't use all 18 tools at once**—start with 2-3 from "Core"
- **Different children need different tools**—what works for one may not work for another
- **Same child may need different tools on different days**—tired/hungry/sick changes everything
- **Your calm is the tool underneath all the other tools**—if you're escalated, none of these work

# **SECTION 5: Common Scenarios - Real Situations + Real Scripts (Pages 16-18)**

## **What To Actually Say When It's Happening**

You know the states. You have the tools. Now here's how to use them in the situations you're actually facing.

Each scenario includes: what it looks like, why it's hard developmentally, and exactly what to do step-by-step with scripts you can use.

### **SCENARIO 1: Bedtime Resistance Meltdown**
**Ages: 3-7 | State: Distress Seeking (High Intensity)**

**What it looks like:**
It's 8pm. You say "Time for bed." Your child starts crying, clinging to you, begging for "5 more minutes," escalating to screaming and unable to calm down.

**Why it's hard:**
Transitions are neurologically difficult for young children. Going from stimulating (play) to non-stimulating (sleep) requires executive function they don't have yet. Add separation anxiety (you leave the room) and bedtime = double threat.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Regulate yourself** (10 seconds)
- Remind yourself: *This is biology, not manipulation*
- Take 2 slow breaths
- Soften your voice before you speak

**Step 2: Get close + validate** (1-2 minutes)
- Kneel to eye level
- Physical contact (hug if they accept it)
- **Script:** "You're really upset about bedtime. You want to keep playing. I know."

**Step 3: Set gentle boundary** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "It's time for sleep. Your body needs rest. I'm here to help you."

**Step 4: Offer choice within boundary** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Do you want to bring your stuffed bear or your blankie to bed?"
- "Should we read one story or two?"

**Step 5: Co-regulate through the transition** (5-10 minutes)
- Stay with them through the process
- **Script:** "We're going to walk to your room together. I'm staying close."
- Slow movements, calm voice, physical touch if they want it

**Step 6: Gentle narration** (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Now we're putting on pajamas. Then we'll brush teeth. Then story time in bed."
- Predictability reduces fear

**Tomorrow:** Warn earlier about bedtime approach. 15 minutes before: "Bedtime is coming soon." 5 minutes: "Almost bedtime—time to finish what you're doing."

### **SCENARIO 2: Sibling Toy Conflict → Hitting**
**Ages: 3-6 | State: Aggression At People**

**What it looks like:**
Two children playing. Suddenly screaming. Your older child (5) just hit the younger one (3) who grabbed their toy.

**Why it's hard:**
Sharing requires impulse control, perspective-taking ("they want it too"), and delayed gratification ("I can have it later"). Ages 3-6 don't have these skills reliably yet. Hitting = communication that "this is mine and I don't have words for this feeling."

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Ensure safety** (5 seconds)
- Move between children physically
- Calm, firm: "No hitting. I'm keeping you both safe."

**Step 2: Brief space** (30 seconds)
- Older child: "Sit here on this cushion. I'll be back in 30 seconds."
- Younger child: Quick comfort—"You got hit. That hurt. You're okay."

**Step 3: Return to aggressor QUICKLY** (within 1-2 minutes)
- Kneel to eye level
- Calm presence (you're not angry at them)
- **Script:** "You hit your sister. You were SO mad she took your toy."

**Step 4: Validate feeling + boundary** (1 minute)
- **Script:** "It's okay to feel angry when someone takes your thing. It's not okay to hit people."
- "Your body felt like it needed to hit. That feeling is real."

**Step 5: Co-regulate** (2-5 minutes)
- Stay close, soft voice
- Wait for breathing to slow
- Physical comfort if they'll accept it

**Step 6: Problem-solve AFTER calm** (5+ minutes later)
- **Script:** "When you feel that mad again, what could you do instead?"
- Offer options: "You could say 'I'm playing with that' or come get me for help."
- NOT: "Say sorry right now" (forced apologies don't teach)

**Step 7: Natural consequence** (if appropriate)
- **Script:** "Your sister felt scared when you hit her. She doesn't want to play near you right now. That's what happens when we hurt people."

**Prevention for next time:** 
Teach BEFORE conflict: "When you don't want to share, you can say 'I'm still using this' or come get me."

### **SCENARIO 3: Public Meltdown - Grocery Store**
**Ages: 3-5 | State: Distress Seeking OR Aggression At Objects**

**What it looks like:**
You said "no" to candy. Your child collapses on the floor, screaming, kicking the cart, people are staring.

**Why it's hard:**
Grocery stores = sensory overload (lights, sounds, people, stimulation everywhere). Prefrontal cortex already taxed. "No" to something they want = overwhelm. Public setting = parental stress increases, making YOUR regulation harder.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Regulate YOURSELF first** (10 seconds)
- **Internal script:** *People's opinions don't matter. My child matters. They need me.*
- Deep breath, drop shoulders, ignore stares

**Step 2: Get to their level** (immediate)
- Kneel on grocery store floor (yes, really)
- Calm, quiet voice (not yelling over their screams)

**Step 3: Validate + stay present** (1-3 minutes)
- **Script:** "You really wanted that candy. You're so disappointed."
- Soft touch on back or shoulder if they'll allow
- DO NOT: Try to reason, threaten consequences, or drag them away

**Step 4: Safety if needed**
- If kicking cart or nearby people: "I'm moving the cart so you don't get hurt."
- Stay kneeling, stay calm

**Step 5: Wait**
- Intensity will peak, then decline (usually 3-8 minutes for young kids)
- Your calm presence speeds this process

**Step 6: When crying slows** (you'll see it)
- **Script:** "You're calming down. Big breaths are helping."
- "I know that was really hard."

**Step 7: Gentle transition** (2 minutes later)
- **Script:** "We're going to finish shopping now. Do you want to sit in the cart or walk next to me?"
- If they're very young or still shaky: Pick them up, carry them, finish quickly

**What NOT to do:**
- Give in to avoid meltdown (teaches: meltdowns get me what I want)
- Threaten: "If you don't stop we're leaving!" (empty threats undermine you)
- Isolate: Taking them to car alone without co-regulation (teaches: I'm alone in my feelings)

**For next time:** 
Prepare before entering store: "We're not buying treats today. I know that's disappointing. If you feel upset, you can tell me and I'll help you feel better."

### **SCENARIO 4: Morning Transition Chaos**
**Ages: 4-7 | State: Aggression At Objects → Distress Seeking**

**What it looks like:**
7:45am. You need to leave for school in 15 minutes. Your child refuses to get dressed, throws shoes, then starts crying that nothing fits right.

**Why it's hard:**
Morning = low blood sugar + tired brain + executive function demands (sequence tasks, make decisions, transition). Clothing sensory issues common in this age. Time pressure from parent = child feels your stress = their stress increases.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Pause + regulate yourself** (10 seconds)
- **Internal:** *We might be late. That's not a crisis. They need co-regulation more than punctuality right now.*
- Breathe, slow down internally

**Step 2: Get to their level** (immediate)
- Sit on floor where they are
- **Script:** "You're really frustrated. Getting dressed is hard this morning."

**Step 3: Validate + set boundary on throwing** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "It's okay to feel frustrated. I can't let you throw shoes. I'm putting them over here."

**Step 4: Simplify the task** (1 minute)
- **Script:** "Let's just do one thing. Can you put on underwear? Just that. Nothing else yet."
- Break overwhelming task into tiny piece

**Step 5: Offer choice** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Do you want the blue shirt or the red one?"
- Restores sense of control

**Step 6: Co-regulate through completion** (5 minutes)
- Stay close, slow pace, acknowledge each small success
- **Script:** "Underwear on—good. Now pants. Just pants."

**Step 7: If still overwhelmed**
- **Script:** "I'm going to help you. I'll hold the shirt, you put your arms through."
- Partnership language

**Prevention for tomorrow:**
- Lay out clothes night before (reduces morning decisions)
- Wake 10 minutes earlier (removes time pressure)
- Offer breakfast BEFORE dressing if low blood sugar is a pattern

### **SCENARIO 5: Friend Conflict Aftermath - "They Don't Like Me"**
**Ages: 5-7 | State: Distress Seeking (Moderate)**

**What it looks like:**
Your child comes home from school crying. Their friend played with someone else at recess. They're saying "Nobody likes me" and "I have no friends."

**Why it's hard:**
Social awareness is increasing (5-7 years) but perspective-taking is still developing. They can't yet understand: "My friend can like me AND play with someone else." Brain interprets: friend plays with other kid = rejection = I'm alone forever. All-or-nothing thinking is developmentally normal.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Validate immediately** (1 minute)
- Sit down, give full attention
- **Script:** "You're really sad. That hurt your feelings when she played with someone else."
- NOT: "You have lots of friends!" (dismisses their current feeling)

**Step 2: Let them talk** (5-10 minutes)
- Active listening: nod, "mm-hmm," reflect back what they say
- **Script:** "So you felt left out. That makes sense."

**Step 3: Acknowledge without fixing** (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Feeling left out is one of the hardest feelings. I've felt that way too."
- Share brief personal example if relevant: "When I was little, my friend once..."

**Step 4: AFTER they feel heard - gentle perspective** (only when calm)
- **Script:** "I'm wondering... does she usually play with you, and today was different?"
- "Do you think she might still like you even though she played with someone else today?"
- Questions, not statements—help them think

**Step 5: Problem-solve if they want** (5 minutes later)
- **Script:** "What could you do tomorrow if this happens again?"
- "Would it help to ask 'Can I play too?' or find a different friend to play with?"

**Step 6: Future orientation** (brief)
- **Script:** "Friendships have hard days sometimes. Tomorrow might feel different."

**What NOT to do:**
- Minimize: "You're overreacting"
- Fix immediately: Calling the other parent to arrange a playdate
- Reason too early: "Well technically she CAN play with other kids" (true, but dismissive of feeling)

### **SCENARIO 7: Refusing to Leave Playground**
**Ages: 3-6 | State: Distress Seeking → Can escalate to Aggression**

**What it looks like:**
Park time is over. You say "Time to go." Your child refuses, starts crying, might throw themselves on ground or hit you when you try to pick them up.

**Why it's hard:**
Stopping fun = loss. Young children can't hold two ideas: "This is ending AND I'll come back another time." Brain reads: fun is over forever = grief response.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Warn in advance** (5 minutes before leaving)
- **Script:** "We're leaving in 5 minutes. You have time for a few more slides."
- Set timer if helpful (visual countdown)

**Step 2: When it's time, validate preemptively** (transition moment)
- **Script:** "I know you don't want to leave. Leaving is hard when you're having fun."

**Step 3: If they refuse/melt down**
- Kneel down, calm presence
- **Script:** "You're really sad we're leaving. You wish we could stay longer."
- DO NOT: Argue about why you have to leave, threaten consequences

**Step 4: If they hit you during this**
- Boundary: "I can't let you hit me." (block hits calmly)
- **Script:** "You're SO mad. Your body feels big. It's still not okay to hit."
- Brief space if needed (step back 2 feet, stay visible, calm)

**Step 5: Offer choice** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "We are leaving. You can walk to the car or I can carry you. Which feels better?"
- Both options lead to same outcome (leaving), but restores sense of control

**Step 6: Follow through calmly**
- If they won't choose: "I'm going to carry you." (pick up calmly, gently)
- **Script while carrying:** "I know you're upset. We'll come back another day."

**Step 7: Co-regulate in car** (5 minutes)
- Let them cry if needed
- **Script:** "You can be sad about leaving. That feeling is okay."
- Don't lecture. Just presence.

**Prevention:**
Always give 5-minute warning. Build in "goodbye ritual" (wave to playground, "See you next time!"). Makes endings predictable.

### **SCENARIO 8: Homework Frustration - "I Can't Do It!"**
**Ages: 6-7 | State: Distress Seeking → Can become Aggression At Objects**

**What it looks like:**
Homework time. Your child tries a math problem, gets it wrong, says "I can't do this!" Starts crying, might crumple the paper or throw pencil.

**Why it's hard:**
Cognitive load + performance pressure + fear of failure. Ages 6-7 are developing academic skills but also acute awareness of "right vs wrong answers." Brain interprets mistake as threat. Frustration tolerance still developing.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Pause the task immediately** (5 seconds)
- **Script:** "Let's stop for a minute. This is feeling too hard right now."

**Step 2: Validate + normalize** (30 seconds)
- Get to their eye level
- **Script:** "You're really frustrated. This problem is tricky. That makes sense."
- "Everyone gets stuck on hard things. Even me."

**Step 3: Co-regulate** (2-5 minutes)
- Sit next to them (not across—same team positioning)
- Calm presence, slow breathing
- **Script:** "Let's both take some slow breaths. We're not in a hurry."

**Step 4: Complexity breaking** (when calmer)
- **Script:** "Let's do this one tiny step at a time. What's the first number in this problem?"
- "Just that. Don't worry about the rest yet."

**Step 5: Partnership language**
- **Script:** "We can figure this out together. I'm here if you need help."
- NOT: "You should know this" or "Try harder"

**Step 6: Process praise** (when they make progress)
- **Script:** "You kept trying even when it was frustrating. That took real effort."

**Prevention:**
Build in breaks every 10-15 minutes. Offer snack before homework (low blood sugar = low frustration tolerance). Notice their capacity that day—if they're exhausted, shorten homework time.

### **SCENARIO 9: Screen Time Ending Meltdown**
**Ages: 3-7 | State: Distress Seeking OR Aggression At Objects**

**What it looks like:**
Screen time is over. You turn off the tablet/TV. Your child screams, might throw the device, hits the couch, or sobs inconsolably.

**Why it's hard:**
Screen = high dopamine stimulation. Turning it off = withdrawal feeling (brain wants that stimulation back). Transition from high stimulation to low = neurologically jarring. Feels like loss.

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Warn before it ends** (5 + 2 minute warnings)
- 5 minutes before: "Screen time ends in 5 minutes. Almost time to stop."
- 2 minutes: "2 more minutes, then all done."
- Set visible timer if helpful

**Step 2: When you turn it off** (transition moment)
- Calm, matter-of-fact tone
- **Script:** "Screen time is over now. I'm turning it off."
- DO NOT: Apologize, negotiate, or explain excessively

**Step 3: Expect upset + validate** (immediate)
- **Script:** "You're really mad I turned it off. You wanted to keep watching."
- Get close (kneel down)

**Step 4: If aggression at objects** (throwing device/hitting furniture)
- Boundary: "I can't let you throw the tablet. I'm putting it up high."
- **Script:** "You're SO frustrated. Your body feels big and angry."

**Step 5: Offer physical discharge option** (1 minute)
- **Script:** "Your body has lots of energy. Do you want to jump on the trampoline or run around outside?"
- Redirect intensity to safe outlet

**Step 6: Co-regulate through the feeling** (3-10 minutes)
- Stay close, let them be upset
- **Script:** "I'm here while you have this big feeling. It's okay to be mad."
- Patient waiting—the feeling WILL pass

**Step 7: When calm** (10+ minutes later)
- **Script:** "That was really hard for you. Tomorrow screen time will end again, and I'll help you with the feeling."

**Prevention:**
Consistent limits (same screen time daily = predictable). Use timer so YOU'RE not the "bad guy" (timer says stop, not parent). Offer engaging alternative immediately after (go outside, play game together).

### **SCENARIO 10: "It's Not Fair!" Protest**
**Ages: 5-7 | State: Distress Seeking (Moderate)**

**What it looks like:**
Sibling got something they didn't. Or a rule applies to them but not to older sibling. Your child is crying, yelling "It's not fair!" and won't let it go.

**Why it's hard:**
Ages 5-7 develop ACUTE awareness of fairness but can't yet understand: different ages = different needs/rules. Brain sees: they got X, I didn't = injustice = threat to my place in family. Concrete thinking (can't grasp nuance).

**What to do:**

**Step 1: Validate the feeling** (immediate)
- **Script:** "You're really upset. It feels unfair that she got to [X] and you didn't."
- NOT: "Life's not fair, get used to it" (true, but dismissive)

**Step 2: Acknowledge their logic** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "You're thinking: she got it, so I should too. That makes sense to you."

**Step 3: Brief explanation** (only if they're calm enough)
- **Script:** "She's 8 and you're 5. Sometimes different ages mean different rules."
- "When you're 8, this rule will be different for you too."
- Keep it short—long lectures don't work

**Step 4: If they're still upset** (1-2 minutes)
- **Script:** "I hear that you're still angry about this. That feeling is real."
- Active listening, stay close

**Step 5: Future orientation** (when calmer)
- **Script:** "This feels really unfair right now. That feeling won't last forever."

**Step 6: Redirect if still stuck**
- **Script:** "I know you're upset about this. Do you want to [alternative activity] or stay close to me for a bit?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Over-explain or justify repeatedly (creates power struggle)
- Change rule to appease (teaches: protest gets me what I want)
- Dismiss: "You're being ridiculous"

# **SECTION 6: Troubleshooting - When It's Not Working (Pages 19-20)**

---

## **Common Problems + Solutions**

You're trying co-regulation. Sometimes it works. Sometimes it doesn't. Here's why—and what to do about it.

---

### **PROBLEM 1: "I Can't Stay Calm When They're Screaming"**

**Why this is hard:**
Your own nervous system responds to their dysregulation. Your amygdala activates (threat detection). Biologically, you're wired to REACT to a screaming child.

**What's actually happening:**
You're getting dysregulated BY their dysregulation. You can't regulate them if you're escalated.

**Solutions:**

**Immediate (in the moment):**
- Take 60-second break if safe (bathroom, outside, different room)
- Hand off to partner if available: "I need a minute. Can you cover?"
- Physical reset: splash cold water on face, shake out your body
- **Internal script:** *Their dysregulation is not an emergency. I can handle this. Slow down.*

**Long-term:**
- Notice YOUR triggers: What situations make you lose it? (public meltdowns? hitting? defiance?)
- Build your own regulation capacity: therapy, exercise, sleep, support system
- Practice on low-stakes moments (when they whine about dinner, practice staying calm)
- **Reality:** You won't be calm every time. Repair when you mess up. (See "repair script" below)

---

### **PROBLEM 2: "They Escalate MORE When I Stay Calm"**

**Why this happens:**
They're testing whether you'll stay steady. If your previous pattern was: you yell → they stop (out of fear), your NEW calm confuses them. They push harder to see if you'll crack.

**What's actually happening:**
**Extinction burst**—when a behavior that used to work suddenly doesn't, the behavior temporarily gets WORSE before it gets better. This is normal. This is expected. This means it's working.

**Solutions:**

**What to do:**
- **Keep going**—do NOT return to yelling
- Stay calm even when intensity increases
- **Internal script:** *This is the extinction burst. This is temporary. Consistency builds trust.*
- **Script to child:** "I'm staying calm even though you're very upset. I'm not going anywhere."

**Timeline:**
Expect 1-2 weeks of "testing." If you stay consistent, intensity will decrease. Inconsistency (calm sometimes, yell other times) makes it worse and longer.

---

### **PROBLEM 3: "This Takes Forever—Punishment Was Faster"**

**Why you're feeling this:**
Punishment stops behavior through fear. Fast results. Co-regulation builds capacity through repetition. Slow results.

**What's actually happening:**
You're comparing different goals:
- Punishment goal: Stop this behavior right now (short-term compliance)
- Co-regulation goal: Build emotional regulation capacity (long-term skill)

**The truth:**
Punishment IS faster in the moment. But it doesn't teach regulation. It teaches: hide your emotions, fear the adult, comply to avoid consequences.

**Solutions:**

**Mindset shift:**
- You're not managing behavior. You're building their nervous system.
- Fast ≠ better. Effective = better.
- **Timeline:** Co-regulation takes 2-4 weeks to see consistent changes. Stick with it.

**Realistic expectations:**
- First week: Feels impossible, you'll want to quit
- Week 2-3: Small changes (meltdowns slightly shorter, they recover faster)
- Week 4+: Noticeable difference (they start asking for help, can sometimes self-soothe)

---

### **PROBLEM 4: "My Partner/Parents Undermine This Approach"**

**Why this happens:**
Your family members see co-regulation as "too soft." They say: "You're spoiling them," "They need real consequences," "We turned out fine being spanked."

**What's actually happening:**
They're defending the methods used on them. Examining parenting = examining their own childhood trauma. That's threatening.

**Solutions:**

**What you CAN control:**
- YOUR response to your child
- Modeling co-regulation consistently
- **Script to family:** "I'm trying a different approach based on child development research. I need you to support that even if you don't agree."

**What you CAN'T control:**
- Their parenting opinions
- Whether they change their methods
- What they do when they're alone with your child

**The reality:**
- Children adapt to different adult styles
- Your child can learn: "Mom does it this way, Grandma does it differently"
- **Consistency from YOU matters more than everyone agreeing**
- Remember: it is YOUR child, your decision and your responsibility. Whatever anyone else says, it's their opinion. You have a final say.

**If your partner is the issue:**
- Share Section 2 of this guide (brain science legitimizes approach)
- Try co-regulation together for 2 weeks as an experiment
- If conflict continues: couples therapy or parenting coach

---

### **PROBLEM 5: "My Child Has ADHD/Autism/Sensory Issues—Does This Still Work?"**

**Short answer: Yes. Even more important for neurodivergent nervous systems.**

**Why:**
Neurodivergent children often have:
- More intense emotional responses (bigger feelings)
- Longer dysregulation time (takes longer to calm)
- Different sensory needs (some want deep pressure, some want zero touch)
- Executive function challenges (harder to sequence "calm down" steps alone)

**Adaptations:**

**For ADHD:**
- Expect longer regulation time (15-30 minutes common)
- Physical movement helps (let them pace, jump, fidget WHILE you stay calm nearby)
- Keep words minimal (their processing is slower when dysregulated)

**For Autism/SPD:**
- Learn THEIR sensory regulation needs (deep pressure? weighted blanket? dim lights? silence?)
- Shutdown is more common—respect need for space while staying nearby
- Visual supports help (emotion chart, timer, pictures of coping strategies)

**For all neurodivergent kids:**
- Co-regulation is THE tool (they need external regulation support longer/more intensely)
- Adjust tools to their nervous system (some need more touch, some need less)
- Validation is critical (they often get message: "your reactions are wrong")

---

### **PROBLEM 6: "I Messed Up and Yelled/Lost My Temper—Now What?"**

**You will mess up. We all do and that's okay. You don't need to be perfect for it to work. The important part is what you do after.**

**What to do (Repair Protocol):**

**Step 1: Regulate yourself first** (5-10 minutes)
- Walk away, calm down, don't try to repair while you're still escalated

**Step 2: Return + name what you did** (1 minute)
- Get to their eye level
- **Script:** "I yelled at you earlier. That wasn't okay. I was feeling overwhelmed and I lost my calm."

**Step 3: Take responsibility** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "That was my mistake. You didn't make me yell. I chose to yell because I didn't use my tools."
- NOT: "You made me so mad that I..." (blame)

**Step 4: Acknowledge impact** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "When I yell, it probably feels scary. I'm sorry I did that."

**Step 5: Future plan** (30 seconds)
- **Script:** "Next time I feel that frustrated, I'm going to [walk away for 60 seconds / take deep breaths] instead of yelling."

**Step 6: Reconnect** (2 minutes)
- **Script:** "Are you okay? Do you want a hug?"
- Physical reconnection if they want it

**Why repair matters:**
- Models: Adults make mistakes, take responsibility, repair relationships
- Teaches: Mistakes don't end connection. Models how to correct.
- **Repairs are MORE powerful teaching moments than "getting it right"**

## **WHEN TO GET PROFESSIONAL HELP**

**Seek support from child psychologist, therapist, or parenting coach if:**

❗ **Child's aggression:**
- Injures others repeatedly despite consistent co-regulation
- Targets same person (you, sibling) with intense aggression daily
- Includes self-harm (hitting self, head-banging)

❗ **Dysregulation duration:**
- Lasts 2+ hours regularly
- Happens 5+ times daily for weeks
- No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent co-regulation

❗ **Your dysregulation:**
- You cannot stay calm despite trying
- You're hitting, yelling, or using physical force regularly
- You feel rage, hatred, or wish to harm your child
- You're so overwhelmed you can't function

❗ **Relationship breakdown:**
- You feel no connection to your child
- Your child seems afraid of you
- Family system is collapsing under stress

❗ **Safety concerns:**
- Child hurts siblings badly enough to need medical care
- You fear you will hurt your child
- Domestic violence in the home

**Resources:**
- Child therapist specializing in ages 3-7
- Parenting coach trained in co-regulation approaches
- Your own therapist (parent mental health = child mental health)
- Parent support groups (other parents using these methods)

# **SECTION 7: Implementation - How To Actually Start (Page 20)**

---

## **You Have The Information. Now What?**

Reading this guide won't change anything. **Using it will.**

Here's how to move from knowing to doing.

---

### **THIS WEEK: Start Small**

**Pick ONE scenario you face regularly:**
- Bedtime battles?
- Morning chaos?
- Sibling fights?
- Public meltdowns?

**Pick TWO tools from Section 4:**
- Calm Presence (always)
- + Validation OR Physical Contact OR Boundary Holding (depending on your scenario)

**Use the 3 R's protocol (Section 1) for that ONE situation this week.**

That's it. Don't try to fix everything at once.

---

### **TRACK WHAT CHANGES**

Answer these questions after 1 week:

**Dysregulation duration:**
- How long do meltdowns last now vs. before you started?
- Are they getting shorter? (Even 2 minutes shorter = progress)

**Your regulation:**
- Are YOU staying calmer?
- Even 1 less yelling episode this week = success

**Child's awareness:**
- Can your child name their emotions yet? ("I'm angry" / "I feel sad")
- Are they starting to ask for help? ("I need a hug" / "I'm feeling big")

**Don't expect:**
- Zero meltdowns
- Perfect compliance
- Immediate transformation

**Do expect:**
- Slightly shorter dysregulation time
- Moments where it works beautifully
- Moments where it fails completely (that's normal—keep going)

### **WEEKS 2-4: Build Gradually**

**Add 1-2 more tools** from Section 4

**Apply to a second scenario** you face regularly

**Notice patterns:**
- What time of day is hardest? (tired = more dysregulation)
- What triggers are predictable? (transitions, hunger, overstimulation)
- Which tools work best for YOUR child?

**Adjust:**
- Tools that don't work for your kid = skip them
- Tools that work = use them more
- Every child is different—adapt
